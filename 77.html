OpenAI's GPT-4o & AI Companions
Seth Bloomberg

May 17, 2024 ⋅  6 min read

Earlier this week, OpenAI showcased its spring updates. While some were speculating on a GPT-5 announcement, the update ultimately did not include a new foundational model but was largely associated with improvements to the ChatGPT product and the GPT-4 foundational model.

Nonetheless, the latest iteration of the GPT-4 model, dubbed GPT-4o, provides a notable step up in performance, accessibility, and multi-modality usage (i.e., text, audio, vision):

  • The system can respond to audio prompts within 200-300 milliseconds. OpenAI claims it’s “2x” faster than previous versions.
  • OpenAI is lowering the cost to access it by half and increasing the rate limits by 5x relative to GPT-4 Turbo.
  • The ChatGPT product will integrate with the text and image functionality, and GPT-4o will be available for free-tier users.

In addition to the spring updates, it appears increasingly likely that OpenAI’s ChatGPT will soon be integrated with Apple’s iOS.

OpenAI’s focus on increasing the underlying model capabilities by layering on performant multi-modal functionality and seeking greater distribution via Apple suggests that it’s likely leaning into creating a consumer-focused product.

However, while this may be somewhat of a shift for OpenAI, it’s just a singular data point indicative of a larger, rapidly growing market—consumer AI.

Consumer AI and Companionship

Consumer AI is a vast and growing space. The focus here, however, will primarily be targeted towards a specific segment of the overall market: AI companions.

Within the Web2 context, Character.ai is one of the more well-known AI companion apps. It allows users to create and interact with characters, which are integrated with and powered by various large language models (LLMs). So, what makes Character.ai and other AI companion applications so compelling from a market perspective? Two reasons stand out: monetization and engagement.

Beyond AI companion applications, the broader generative AI application category has shown an ability to monetize more quickly than its non-AI counterparts and retain paying customers at a rate comparable to or even higher than its non-AI counterparts. Specifically, AI companion applications also show a high propensity for engagement.

Among generative AI consumer mobile applications, character.ai showed more than three times the engagement of the second-leading application, which is also within the AI companion sector (albeit targeting enterprises rather than consumers). Engaging and retentive applications, especially in the consumer domain, lead to a higher probability of acting as a user's “primary interface”, which is a key competitive differentiator.

Translating this into the AI x Crypto domain, many teams have noted this growing market and are developing AI companion applications.

AI x Crypto Companion Applications

One of these upstarts is MyShell. It enables users to create chatbot-style companions and has raised over $15 million from prominent investors. The team is working to incorporate voice capabilities into its platform, which should improve engagement and general application functionality. The SHELL token will eventually play a critical role within the application, such as accessing premium features.

In addition to MyShell, Virtuals protocol is building on Base focused on allowing users to create “diverse AI entities capable of text, voice and motion…for various virtual environments such as games or online spaces.” The project has a noted focus on animation and is developing “audio-to-animation” capabilities.

Similar to Character.ai, Dippy is developing a “role-playing” AI application. The application is mobile-friendly, and users can select different companion types (e.g., friends, therapists, etc.) to interact with.

Open-Source and Ownable AI Models

The projects noted above (and numerous others) highlight two broader themes and ethos attached to many projects within the AI x Crypto sector: high value is placed on open-source vs. closed-source models, and an emphasis is also placed on model ownership capabilities.

A common thread running through MyShell, Virtuals, and Dippy is their use of Bittensor. For the purposes of these projects, Bittensor serves as a means of bootstrapping specific, open-source AI models.

Within the Bittensor ecosystem, teams can launch subnets, which are self-contained environments that incentivize the development of AI models. Bittensor has already done well at attracting top ML and AI talent, making it an attractive option for teams to outsource model development.

  • MyShell’s Bittensor subnet is focused on developing premier text-to-speech (TTS) models.
  • The Virtuals protocol’s subnet is aimed at building audio-to-animation (A2A) models. The team sees prompting AI companions via voice or general audio as a key component of these sophisticated companions.
  • Dippy’s subnet is specifically incentivizing the development of “roleplaying” models.

The intention behind the push for open-source models is to, at a minimum, provide a viable alternative to closed-source and proprietary models. Closed-source models, such as those produced by OpenAI, create massive centralization risks. As AI becomes more pervasive and infused into individuals' daily lives, it will inevitably shape the lens through which broader society understands the world. If this underlying AI system is controlled by a single, centralized entity (e.g., OpenAI), it would have immense power.

Beyond just open-sourcing AI models, Ora protocol is developing a framework to enable model ownership. The architecture is still nascent, but the goal is to effectively tokenize AI models via ERC-20 (and other similar token standards) to enable broad ownership capabilities.

Economies of Scale and the Challenges of Open-Source Alignment

Consumer AI, and specifically AI companion applications, have tremendous promise regarding engagement, retention, and monetization. More broadly, AI venture investment has hit a massive scale, with ~$20 billion already invested so far this year. Taken together, these facts imply this sector will not slow down anytime soon. This bodes well for both pure AI companies and those building within the AI x Crypto domain.

However, many AI x Crypto applications' reliance on open-source models could also hinder their ability to go to market with a performant product. For example, OpenAI and Character.ai have raised massive funds and generally benefit from economies of scale. This enables them to accumulate and devote vast resources to their proprietary products in addition to driving distribution partnerships with giants like Apple.

Teams building within the AI x Crypto sector will have to wrestle with the potential performance gains stemming from closed-source models versus aligning with the broader ethos of open-source and ownable models. With this in mind, we may see a variety of outcomes. Some teams may double down on alignment with the open-source ethos, and some may opt for a hybrid approach, allowing users to leverage both open and closed-source models but potentially incentivizing the use of the former.
