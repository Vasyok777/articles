Off The Grid - Gunning For Mass Adoption

Andrew Dyer
Sunny Shi
Chris Davis

Oct 18, 2024   8 min read

Primer

After over $9 billion invested in the Web3 gaming industry since 2015, the sector has finally achieved what some thought impossible: a truly AAA Web3 game.

After five years in development, Gunzilla Games' Off the Grid officially launched in early access on PC and console on October 8. Since then, over 6 million addresses have been created on the GUNZ testnetGunzilla's Avalanche L1reaching a peak of nearly 900,000 DAUs on October 11.

Though theres not yet a liquid token to gain direct exposure to this success, the market has responded by driving up the price of $PERCthe token of a DAO that purchased $250,000 of nodes on the GUNZ networkby over 500%.

Given the impressive growth metrics and positive market response, its safe to say Off the Grid is off to a strong start.

Off the Grids go-to-market strategy has heavily leveraged influencer marketing. At its peak on Friday, October 10, it reached over 150,000 concurrent Twitch viewers, driven by major gaming streamers like Ninja, TimTheTatman, and Shroud. Some streamers have directly addressed the game's Web3 components, explaining these concepts to Web2 gamers in a digestible and convincing way. Given many gamers' historically negative stance on Web3, positive sentiment from these influencers could significantly shift gamers perception of Web3 gaming in a more favorable direction and drive further adoption.

However, the strength of this impact depends on how well Off the Grid can sustain gamers attention. Since the end of paid streams on October 12, the numbers have steadily declined, falling to just 7,000 peak concurrent viewers on October 15. While their marketing strategy has succeeded in capturing initial interest, the real challenge is converting that interest into lasting engagement and player retention.

Despite the decline in Twitch viewership, daily transactions on the network continue to make all-time highs, indicating growing activity within the game. With Call of Duty: Black Ops 6 set to release on October 25, well soon witness a test of how strong interest in Off the Grid truly is.

Another major long-term challenge for Off the Grid is maintaining a stable in-game economy. The ecosystem's utility token, $GUN, has not yet launched, and the impact of earning $GUN in-game, spending it to mint items, and transacting on the marketplace remains to be seen.

While Web2 gamers have responded positively to the game so far, the sustainability of the economy could significantly influence the games future success. If their model proves unsustainable or enjoyability declines, it may reinforce the belief held by many gamers that introducing financial assets detracts from the traditional gaming experience.

So, given the context of Off the Grids promising start and the challenges ahead, some questions worth considering are:

  • Will Off the Grid be able to translate its initial momentum into long-term player retention over the coming months?
  • Can Off the Grid gain enough traction to compete with well-established shooter franchises such as Call of Duty and Fortnite?
  • How will the success or failure of Off the Grid impact the greater Web3 gaming industry?
  • Will Off the Grids in-game economy prove sustainable in the long run?

Andrew

Lessons from Big Time

Big Time is a multiplayer action RPG created by the former CEO of Decentraland and several AAA game developers. It conducted its Token Generation Event and Coinbase listing in October of last year. The game ran a preseason leaderboard airdrop in which around 100 million tokens were distributed to top players at the end of each month for three months. Players could climb the leaderboard by leveling up, sharing referral codes, doing premium quests, and forging items. This initial token incentive led to a flurry of Twitch activity just around the TGE that has since flat-lined:

The first rewards and aggressively high FDV led to a 60% price drop off the initial listing (from around $.35 to $.14). However, $BIGTIME eventually rallied to $1 in December amid a wave of large rallies across new token listings. At these prices, optimized farming strategies involving renting in-game assets became extremely lucrative. For example, some youtubers estimated efficient runs could earn around 50-100 tokens per hour of play. Given that some users are claiming you may soon be able to earn a similar amount in $GUN tokens for an hour of play, it is probably wise to be wary. Big Times high initial rewards eventually proved to be fleeting as prices have since reverted to their post-listing lows. Given this context, some thoughts and lessons for the future Off the Grid ecosystem:

  • The pace of initial token rewards matter - too high a payout may attract more value-extractor-type players who rush to sell their rewards, perhaps stalling the games traction and flywheel. At $.63 back in December, Big Time was going to pay out six figures in rewards to its top players right from the get-go - does that sound sustainable?
  • Token sink types will affect sustainability - for Big Time, $BIGTIME is used to craft items, enter Prestige dungeons (higher chance of rare rewards), and purchase cosmetic upgrades (that may gate-keep certain gameplay). Its debatable whether this has proven sustainable or not given the price action. Meanwhile, OtG requires users to purchase a Pro subscription, which costs ~$12/month, to access the in-game marketplace, which may be a more effective token sink.
  • Abstracting crypto rails will improve user growth - traditional gamers are not known for liking crypto, and they have been known to dunk on NFTs in particular. To purchase Big Time NFTs, users have to interact with their partnered marketplace Open Loot, purchase a collectible, move them to in-game, and then equip them. In contrast, one can play OtG without ever interacting with an NFT marketplace if they so choose. This design choice will likely be beneficial in the long run.

Sunny Shi

Off the Grid found standout success on launch day and the game itself looks up to par with the AAA standards of the modern gamer. The path forward, however, is a challenging one.

Offchain Challenges - Staying Relevant

With 900,000 DAUs, Off the Grid (OTG) is off to an impressive start, even by Web2s standards. However, the competitive shooter genre is notoriously crowded, presenting OTG with significant hurdles when it comes to maintaining long-term relevance among Web2 gamers. A recent case study can be found in The Finals, a fast-paced FPS from Embark Studios (the creators of Battlefield), which attracted over 200,000 concurrent players on Steam shortly after its stealth launch towards the end of 2023. Like OTG, The Finals wasnt widely known pre-launch and gained early momentum through initial Twitch streams and its engaging, unique gameplay elements. Despite being the talk of the town for a few weeks, the game slowly bled users and never saw another peak in activity, now down to just ~20,000 concurrent players.

On the other hand, Apex Legends, another Battle-Royale style shooter, started off with ~100,000 concurrent players in 2020, and slowly grew their player base for 3 years, peaking at over 600,000 concurrent players in early 2023.

When evaluating OTG's prospects, it seems more plausible that its engagement curve will look closer to that of The Finals. Apex entered the market as a fresh contender in the then-nascent Battle Royale genre, bolstered by significant expertise and resources through its publisher, Electronic Arts (EA). As the shooter landscape in gaming tends to be incredibly top-heavy, expecting 900,000 DAUs to hold for an extended period of time is a tall ask.

However, even The Finals 20,000 concurrent players would be an extraordinary achievement for a GameFi project, especially when considering most crypto-native titles struggle to attract even 1,000 daily unique active wallets. OTGs current onchain wallet metrics already place it within the top 10 most played Blockchain games according to DappRadar.

Onchain Challenges - Maintaining Utility

When it comes to GameFi tokens, the death blow to sustainable price action tends to be inflation without consumption. As Web3 gamers are profit-oriented, more and more tokens need to be emitted to incentivize activity. At the same time, users refuse to spend on their experience unless doing so can extract more value, which limits token utility. In contrast, traditional gamers can spend hundreds of dollars a year on digital products within their favorite games, whether it's to unlock battle passes or cosmetics.

With this in mind, I believe there are only two ways for a GameFi ecosystem to be sustainable:

  1. Zero-sum tokenomics where losing players pay out winning players.
  2. A value driven GameFi economy where players are willing to spend money for digital goods to improve their gaming experience, without the expectation of profit.

To date, no GameFi protocol has successfully addressed the challenge of creating sustainable token economies. Andrew has already mentioned token "sinks" as a potential solution, where organic player spending helps manage supply growth and enhance in-game utility. For this to work, the game must provide a compelling enough experience to motivate players to spend. In essence, the game itself needs to be the product, not the token. Going off of the initial footage, Off the Grids game already looks incredibly engaging, and I believe the title represents a leading candidate to break the mold. Just be mindful that for the genre, maintaining users over multi-year period tends to be incredibly rare.
