The Week In Crypto Policy: NDAA Amendments, Senate Approps Hearing
George Leonardo

Jun 14, 2024   7 min read

Key Insights

  • The House is considering two blockchain amendments to the NDAA, including one already approved by voice vote that would establish a working group focused on blockchain and smart contracts within the DoD.
    • Votes on further amendments and final passage are expected later today.
  • At a Senate Appropriations hearing, Senator Bill Hagerty (R-TN) urged SEC Chair Gensler to provide regulatory clarity and approve spot Ether ETPs, Senator Chris Van Hollen (D-MD) keyed in on illicit finance concerns, and Senator Dick Durbin (D-IL) expressed skepticism about giving the CFTC oversight authority over the crypto market.
  • Former President Trump met with Bitcoin miners at Mar-a-Lago and said he wants all Bitcoin to be made in the USA.
  • President Biden's campaign is considering accepting crypto donations.

NDAA Amendments

This week, the House is considering the National Defense Authorization Act. Among 350 other amendments, two blockchain-related amendments made it out of the Rules Committee for consideration on the House floor.

One amendment was approved by voice vote en bloc (i.e., approved alongside a bundle of several other amendments) on Wednesday. The other is expected to be voted on as part of another bundle of amendments later today.

You can track amendments here.

Here's a closer look:

Amdt. 256

  • Sponsor: Rep. Kat Cammack (R-FL)
  • Vote: Approved by Voice Vote (En Bloc 1).
  • Summary: This amendment would direct the Secretary of Defense to develop a working group to identify whether blockchain and smart contracts could improve functions and efficiency at the DoD.
  • Text: Amdt. 256.

Amdt. 1012

  • Sponsors: Zach Nunn (R-IA), Abigail Spanberger (D-VA), Jen Kiggans (R-VA), Don Davis (D-NC), Chris Smith (R-NJ), and Brad Wenstrup (R-OH)
  • Vote: To Be Considered with En Bloc 4 amendments, likely later today.
  • Summary: This amendment would prohibit the Department of Defense from entering into any contracts for any equipment, system, or service that uses certain blockchain technology or equipment "of or originating from a foreign adversary."
    • Foreign adversaries include: China, Cuba, Iran, North Korea, Russia, and Venezuelan politician Nicolas Maduro (See full definition here: 17 C.F.R. 7.2.)
    • Specific companies and networks listed as subject to the ban include:
      • The Blockchain-based Services Network
      • The Spartan Network
      • The Conflux Network
      • iFinex, Inc.
      • Red Date Technology Co., Ltd.
  • Text: Amdt. 1012.

What's Next?

The House is expected to vote on further amendments and final passage of the bill later today. The Senate is still working through its own version of an NDAA. For a bill to be presented to the President, it must pass the House and Senate in identical form, so the House and Senate will ultimately need to reconcile differences between their respective bills. Notably, Rep. Cammack's amendment was included in last year's House-passed NDAA, but it was excluded from the final conferenced bill that was signed into law.

Senate Appropriations Committee Hearing

On Thursday, the Senate Appropriations Committee held a hearing to discuss funding for the SEC and CFTC.

Key Crypto Takeaways:

  • Senator Bill Hagerty (R-TN) pressed SEC Chair Gensler to provide more clarity for crypto market participants and to approve spot Ether ETPs.
  • Chair Gensler echoed previous statements before Congress, asserting that crypto is rife with fraud and abuse, most cryptoassets are securities, and that compliance, not clarity, is the problem.
    • He expects spot Ether ETP registration review to be complete by summer, but he stopped short of explicitly stating whether Ether is a commodity.
  • Subcommittee Chair Chris Van Hollen (D-MD) appeared open to legislation addressing crypto's jurisdictional issues, but it made clear addressing consumer protection and AML concerns would be two top priorities.
  • Senator Dick Durbin (D-IL) is skeptical of ceding crypto oversight authority to the CFTC, emphasizing differences in size and resources compared to the larger SEC.

Here's a closer look at some of the crypto-related exchanges.

Senator Bill Hagerty (R-TN): Regulatory Clarity & ETH ETPs

Regulatory Clarity

Senator Hagerty argued the SEC should prioritize constructive rules of the road for the crypto industry instead of allocating resources to rulemakings "way beyond its remit" (citing, for example, last weeks 5th Circuit decision vacating the SECs private funds rule).

Hagerty also argued that the U.S. should be a leader in this industry, but the regulatory roadblocks and uncertainty are stifling innovation.

In a later exchange, Hagerty suggested that if the U.S. had clear rules in place, FTX may have been less likely to operate offshore and U.S. customers would have been better protected.

Spot Ether ETP

Senator Hagerty asked both Chairs to answer whether they thought Ether was a commodity.

  • Chair Behnam: Yes.
  • Chair Gensler did not answer directly. Instead, he pointed to the SECs recent approval of spot Ether ETP 19b-4 applications and previous approvals of Ether futures ETFs.
  • According to Gensler, staff is still reviewing issuers' disclosures and registrations, but he expects that process will be completed this summer.

Senator Chris Van Hollen (D-MD): Enforcement & Illicit Finance

Senator Van Hollen asked how a House appropriations bill's provision to defund certain SEC enforcement efforts involving digital assets would impact the agency's ability to be a cop on the beat.

  • Gensler said the measure would undercut ongoing enforcement actions, while also flagging issues he sees in the crypto market generally.
    • More specifically, Gensler says customers currently lack adequate disclosures from crypto token issuers.
    • He also argued registration requirements for intermediaries under existing securities laws protect customers from risks such as the commingling of funds and conflicts of interest.

AML/KYC

Van Hollen asked both Chairs how Congress should deal with AML issues in crypto.

  • Chair Behnam responded that if the CFTC were given more authority over the crypto spot market, AML/KYC would be a key component.
    • He added that Congress would not have to stray too far from existing laws, while also noting the CFTC already works with FinCEN on illicit finance issues under existing authorities.
  • Chair Gensler said Bitcoin is the token of choice for ransomware actors.
    • He also raised concerns with crypto tokens being transferable outside of the traditional financial system on permissionless ledgers.

Senator Dick Durbin (D-IL): Skeptical of Crypto and CFTC Oversight

Senator Durbin, who is also the Senate Democratic Whip, asked Chair Behnam:

  • What in the world makes you think you can get into this fast-moving capacious world and be effective as a regulator?"
  • Chair Behnam responded by noting the CFTC has been an effective regulator under its existing authorities. And if Congress expanded the agency's authority over the crypto spot market, he would expect Congress to also provide the agency with any necessary resources.
  • Chair Gensler argued most of crypto involves issuers asking the public to invest in securities, so the SEC's disclosure-based regime is a natural fit. He also noted the SEC has roughly nine times the staff as the CFTC.

In closing, Durbin compared crypto to a Chia Pet: It looks like a good idea for a gift until you buy it.

Financial Regulator Nominations

President Biden announced his intent to nominate CFTC Commissioner Christy Goldsmith Romero to replace Martin Gruenberg as FDIC Chair.

Courts
