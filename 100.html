The Week In Crypto Policy: Mid-Year Recap & Look Ahead
George Leonardo

Jun 21, 2024 ⋅  10 min read

Key Insights

  • Throughout the 118th Congress, Members of Congress have crafted legislation covering stablecoins, market structure, illicit finance, commerce, tax, SEC accounting rules, and CBDCs.
  • With time running out for bills to get enacted before a new Congress gets sworn in, Members will look to reach bipartisan compromises and combine legislation with "must pass" bills to get their priorities across the finish line.
  • As the House has already advanced FIT 21 with massive bipartisan support, eyes are now primarily on the Senate to see whether bipartisan momentum on crypto legislation continues this Congress.
  • For comprehensive legislation addressing stablecoins and market structure to gain momentum in the Senate, measures to address illicit finance concerns of key Senators would likely need to be added.

Overview

The recap below provides a high-level review of key bills pending in Congress on the stablecoin, market structure, illicit finance, commerce, tax, SAB 121, and CBDC fronts. Links to more detailed summaries and texts are included throughout for those interested in diving deeper.

Stablecoins

There are currently two main stablecoin bills. In the House, Chair Patrick McHenry’s Clarity for Payment Stablecoins Act passed out of committee by a 31-16 vote last July.

In the Senate, Senators Lummis and Gillibrand unveiled their Lummis-Gillibrand Payment Stablecoin Act earlier this year. It remains pending in the Senate Banking Committee.

At a high level, House and Senate drafts agree that payment stablecoins should be backed by highly liquid, reserve assets on at least a 1:1 basis, issuers should clearly disclose key information about reserve assets and redemption processes, and that there should be minimum federal regulatory requirements for state-approved issuers (a “federal floor”).

Disagreements persist over what exactly that federal floor should look like, how much power the Fed should have over approving and overseeing state issuers, and to what extent non-bank entities should be allowed to issue stablecoins. How to deal with algorithmic stablecoins also remains an open issue.

  • Note: Here’s a more detailed look at key differences between the House and Senate bills by The Digital Chamber.

Look Ahead

House, Senate, and White House aides will continue to work behind the scenes to resolve key differences and finalize a bill that has the best chance of getting signed into law. Measures to address illicit finance concerns may also need to be added to quell certain Senators concerns. As for process, a deal would likely need to hitch a ride on a must-pass bill like the NDAA given the limited remaining floor time. (For context, the House and Senate will be in recess all of August, all of October, and half of November).

Based on previous reporting, it's also possible stablecoin legislation gets paired with Senate Banking Chairman Sherrod Brown's SAFER Banking Act as part of any compromise. SAFER is a key priority for Senator Brown that is primarily focused on cannabis banking reform. But it also includes some language aimed at restricting banking regulators’ ability to debank politically disfavored groups (though it's unclear how effective this provision would be in practice). (See § 10(b) of SAFER; Summary here).

Market Structure

In short, market structure refers to legislation governing key aspects of the crypto market such as clarifying CFTC and SEC jurisdictional lines and regulatory obligations for issuers and intermediaries.

The House recently passed FIT 21 by a 279-136 vote – with 71(!) Democrats voting yes.

Last July, Senators Lummis and Gillibrand reintroduced their Responsible Financial Innovation Act, which remains pending in Senate Finance Committee. (Summary here).

Senate Agriculture Chairwoman Debbie Stabenow (D-MI) has also been workshopping a market structure bill with Ranking Member John Boozman (R-AR), and is engaged with House and Senate leaders on reaching a bipartisan deal.

Look Ahead

All eyes are now on the Senate to see what Senator Stabenow's bill looks like and if/when Senate Finance Chair Ron Wyden (D-OR) takes up any aspects of the Lummis-Gillibrand bill. A Stabenow-Boozman bill could be unveiled soon– and, according to Stabenow, possibly marked up before the August recess.

Key issues to look out for include how far apart a Stabenow/Boozman framework is from FIT 21, how the crypto industry and community respond to any bill text, and how hard crypto-skeptic Democrats push back.

Key challenges for passing weighty market structure legislation include (1) the clock is ticking on remaining floor time and (2) parties may want to wait until after the November elections to see the lay of the land next Congress. For example, if Republicans think they will have a Senate majority and control the White House, will they be willing to compromise with Democrats on comprehensive crypto legislation or prefer to wait until they are in the driver’s seat of negotiations next Congress?

The Ag Committees also have to finalize a farm bill this year, which many members may view as a priority compared to crypto legislation.

  • One final note: Like Rep. McHenry, Senator Stabenow is retiring at the end of this Congress, which may add further motivation for striking a deal this year.

Illicit Finance

For this section, we'll cover each chamber in turn.

Senate

In general, the Senate has been more focused on illicit finance concerns this Congress than their House counterparts. Most recently, language from Senator Warner’s Terrorist Financing Prevention Act passed the Senate Intelligence Committee as part of a must-pass Intelligence Authorization Act. In short, the bill would empower the Treasury secretary to sanction a broadly defined swath of “digital asset transaction facilitators” if they knowingly facilitate a significant financial transaction with terrorists. (Summary here; Text here).

​DAAMLA and CANSEE, two bills that industry has decried as potentially killing DeFi and digital assets in the U.S., have remained stalled in Senate Banking since last year.

Finally, earlier this year, Republican Senators Bill Hagerty (R-TN) and Thom Tillis (R-NC) unveiled draft text for the ENFORCE Act (Summary & text here). While the bill is only in discussion draft form, it shows how key Senate Republicans would prefer to address AML concerns compared to DAAMLA and CANSEE.

Look Ahead

Senator Warner has expressed openness to working with industry to narrow the scope of his bill, so perhaps a slimmed down version more palatable to pro-crypto Members could get included as part of a broader crypto compromise and/or as part of an NDAA/IAA conferenced bill.

Importantly, DAAMLA and CANSEE sponsors are senior influential members (e..g, Sens. Warren (D-MA), Reed (D-RI), Warner (D-VA), Rounds (R-SD)). So to the extent any crypto legislation like stablecoins and market structure start moving, it’s unlikely these Senators would simply sit on the sidelines without seeking some concessions addressing the illicit finance concerns underlying their bills.

House

Rep. Zachary Nunn’s (R-IA) Financial Technology and Protection Act passed 50-0 out of HFSC last July. This bill would establish a financial technology working group to study and develop recommendations for how lawmakers should combat the uses of new financial technologies, such as digital assets, for illicit activity.

At House hearings on illicit finance earlier this year, Members noticed and discussed legislation such as studying illicit activity involving DeFi (included as § 605 of FIT 21), directing Treasury to study risks of privacy-enhancing technology like mixers (see, e.g., § 2(b) of Rep. Casten's Blockchain Integrity Act and a Senate NDAA amendment from last year), and expanding the Secret Service’s authority to go after institutions facilitating illicit finance (Combating Money Laundering in Cyber Crime Act).

Look Ahead

Comparing House and Senate proposals, it appears directing one or more federal agency to study and report to Congress on illicit finance activity involving crypto is the lowest hanging fruit. So perhaps something like Rep. Nunn’s bill - which has already received unanimous committee support – could get rolled into a broader crypto bill or must-pass vehicle.

  • Note: FinCEN is still considering a rule to impose enhanced reporting requirements on U.S. financial instutions for certain transactions involving “CVC mixing,” which has raised serious concerns with industry (Summary of key concerns).

Blockchain/Innovation/Commerce

While Financial Services bills like market structure and stablecoins have garnered most of the media's attention, House Energy & Commerce has quietly pushed forward bills aimed at promoting non-financial use cases for blockchain technology.

Specifically, in May, the House passed three commerce-related blockchain bills under suspension of the rules - meaning they each passed by at least a 2/3 vote and were strongly bipartisan.

Here’s a quick snapshot of the three bills:

  • Consumer Safety Technology Act: a bill to Direct the Department of Commerce and the Federal Trade Commission to study how the use of blockchain technology in commerce may limit fraud and other unfair or deceptive practices.
  • Promoting Resilient Supply Chains Act of 2023: a bill to improve U.S. supply chain resiliency by directing Commerce to develop a national strategy and best practices for ensuring robust supply chains, including by identifying and studying blockchain-based use cases, along with other emerging technologies.
  • Deploying American Blockchains Act of 2023: a bill to direct the Secretary of Commerce to serve as the principal advisor to the President on blockchain policy and to support U.S. leadership when it comes to the deployment, use, and competitiveness of blockchain technology, applications built on blockchains, tokens, and tokenization.
  • More detailed summaries here.

One more note: While not E&C related, Rep. Cammack’s NDAA amendment to direct the Secretary of Defense to establish a working group to study and identify ways blockchain and smart contracts could create efficiencies at DoD passed the House last Friday.

Look Ahead

It’s unclear if and how the Senate will act on these bills. But given the bipartisan margin by which the Energy & Commerce bills passed the House, don't count them out. Their House Democrat sponsors may be able to convince their Senate colleagues to let at least certain provisions hitch a ride on a must-pass bill.

Tax

The scope of Treasury’s proposed rule regarding implementation of digital asset “broker” tax reporting requirements has been a major concern for crypto.

In the House, Chair McHenry and Rep. Ritchie Torres (D-NY) have introduced the Keep Innovation in America Act (11 Cosponsors; 5 Ds), which aims to clarify and limit the scope of who would be considered a broker and what information must be reported under the law.

In the Senate, Senator Lummis's and Gillibrand's bill (§ 802) includes nearly identical language as the Keep Innovation in America Act.

Senate Finance Chair Ron Wyden (D-OR) has also been open to a fix – even sponsoring a clarifying amendment with Senator Lummis and Toomey to limit the scope of reporting requirements in the 117th Congress.

Look Ahead

Given the bicameral, bipartisan support for addressing the issue from key Members in the House and Senate, and the urgency created by Treasury potentially finalizing its proposed rule, passing a legislative fix this Congress is still in play.

SAB 121

While the White House vetoed the SAB 121 repeal resolution and a Congressional override is unlikely, Chair Gensler could always withdraw the rule on his own accord (and/or at the behest of White House/Congressional pressure).

Congress could also invoke its power over the purse by defunding SEC efforts to enforce SAB 121. Indeed, the House included a provision to defund SAB 121 in their FSGG spending bill, but it’s unclear if the Senate would keep such language in any final spending bill.

CBDCs

A few weeks back, the House passed Rep. Emmer's CBDC Anti-Surveillance State Act, which would prohibit the Fed from issuing, developing, or designing a CBDC by a 216-92 vote.

It's unlikely this anti-CBDC bill makes it through the Senate after receiving just 3 Democrat yes votes in the House, but the issue may continue to pop up on the campaign trail. In the unlikely event an anti-CBDC bill were to pass, it would probably be a slimmed down bill focused on clarifying that the Fed can’t issue a CBDC without Congressional approval – rather than an outright ban on even developing a CBDC.
