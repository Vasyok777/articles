House Agriculture Committee

George Leonardo

Jan 3, 2024 ⋅  7 min read

Key Insights

  • The House Agriculture Committee plays a key role on important crypto policy issues, including: defining which cryptoassets are commodities, setting rules for intermediaries, and overseeing the Commodities Futures Trading Commission (“CFTC”).
  • Chairman Glenn Thompson has worked closely with House Financial Services Chairman Patrick McHenry to draft the Financial Innovation and Technology for the 21st Century Act (“FIT 21”).  If FIT 21 or similar market structure legislation gets signed into law, House Ag would continue serving an important oversight role in ensuring the CFTC writes implementation rules consistent with Congressional intent.
  • Members’ top focus next year will likely be reauthorizing the farm bill - a comprehensive authorization bill governing a broad array of federal programs related to agriculture, nutrition, forestry, credit, energy, and more.  The current farm bill expires in September 2024. So, its reauthorization could, in theory, provide a legislative vehicle for crypto provisions - though the prospects for this are uncertain.

House Agriculture Committee

Jurisdiction

The House Agriculture Committee (“House Ag”) has jurisdiction over commodities, forestry, nutrition, livestock, rural development, and of course, agriculture. Moreover, the committee has jurisdiction over commodity markets, such as exchanges, and the Commodities Futures Trading Commission. As a result, House Ag plays a key role in crafting legislation affecting cryptoasset commodities and the exchanges on which they trade.


More specifically, here are some key issues over which the Committee plays an important role:

  • Clarifying which crypto assets are commodities versus securities;
  • Empowering the CFTC to have authority to regulate and oversee the cryptoasset commodity spot market;
  • Setting customer protection rules for crypto exchanges seeking to list cryptoasset commodities;
  • Overseeing the CFTC and the agency’s proposed regulations with a tie to cryptoasset commodities.

Key Legislation & Actions

Financial Innovation and Technology for the 21st Century (“FIT 21”)

The House Ag Committee has jurisdiction over FIT 21 along with the House Financial Services Committee (“HFSC”). At a markup on July 27, 2023, House Ag advanced FIT 21 to the House floor by voice vote, signaling bipartisan support for the legislation. Throughout the markup, Members from both sides of the aisle noted that FIT 21, while not perfect, would be an improvement from the status quo in terms of providing greater clarity for innovators and protections for consumers.  

FIT 21 would amend the Commodity Exchange Act to grant the CFTC spot market authority over digital assets that meet the definition of digital commodity (i.e., are sufficiently decentralized and functional). It also requires digital commodity intermediaries, like exchanges, to register with the CFTC and a self-regulatory organization, much like traditional commodity exchanges. Intermediaries would also have to adhere to anti-fraud and anti-market manipulation rules, keep adequate books and records, prohibit commingling of customer and company funds, and various other rules aimed at ensuring market integrity and protecting customers.

Looking ahead to a potential House floor vote, House Ag Members, particularly Democrats, may look to modify the bill through various amendments. For example, at the markup and previous hearings on FIT 21, Democrats voiced support for stricter proof-of-reserve requirements, provisions that promote diversity and inclusion, studies on the energy and environmental impacts of crypto mining, and ensuring that regulators have adequate enforcement authority during the provisional registration period.

Finally, FIT 21 grants the CFTC (and SEC) broad authority to write additional rules to strengthen and refine statutory requirements. As such, if FIT 21 were to become law, House Ag would still play a substantial role in overseeing the CFTC’s rulemaking process and ensuring the law is enforced in accordance with Congressional intent.

Farm Bill

The “Farm Bill” is not a crypto bill. But it is important to mention because it is often the number one priority of Members on the committee. The Farm Bill is a vast bill that governs a broad array of federal programs from crop insurance to nutrition assistance, forestry management, and more. As such, it unites a broad coalition of Members from various geographic regions and ends of the political spectrum. As a result, the Committee on a whole is often more bipartisan in nature than most committees, including the House Financial Services Committee. Moreover, the Farm Bill must be reauthorized periodically, usually every five years, thus providing a “must pass” bill in authorization years. The most recent Farm Bill was set to expire in 2023, but was extended to September 2024. As one of a few must pass bills in Congress next year, the farm bill could, in theory, serve as a legislative vehicle for non-agriculture specific legislative items.

Key Members

As evidenced by the voice vote on FIT 21 and discussions at previous hearings, crypto debates have been less partisan in House Ag than the House Financial Services Committee or Senate Banking Committee. As such, the breakdown below does not divide between pro-crypto and anti-crypto Members of Congress like previous committee reports, but rather highlights a few of the most influential and engaged committee members on crypto policy.


Chairman Glenn “GT” Thompson (R-PA): As House Ag Chairman, Rep. Thompson played a leading role in drafting sections of FIT 21 governing digital commodities and digital commodity intermediaries. In fact, sections of FIT 21 governing CFTC authority and digital commodity intermediaries are substantially similar to Chair Thompson’s Digital Commodity Exchange Act introduced in the 117th Congress. For example, both bills would amend the Commodity Exchange Act to grant the CFTC jurisdiction over the digital commodity spot market and require digital commodity intermediaries to comply with the CEA’s core principles. Chairman Thompson’s continued efforts to build support amongst House Ag members will be key to boosting bipartisan support for FIT 21 on the House floor.


Rep. Dusty Johnson (R-SD): Rep. Johnson is the Chair of the House Ag subcommittee with jurisdiction over digital commodities. As such, he can use his position to hold hearings on crypto-related issues. Further, as a cosponsor of FIT 21, he has helped steer support for enacting a clear regulatory framework for crypto. Earlier this year, he introduced a resolution expressing support for blockchain technology and digital assets. The resolution reads in part: “Congress should enact a functional framework tailored to the specific risks of different digital asset-related activities and unique benefits of distributed ledger technology, distributed networks, and decentralized systems.” In the 117th Congress, he cosponsored a bill to bring crypto holdings in line with Congressional ethics rules by requiring Members to disclose crypto holdings and transactions above a certain threshold.


Ranking Member David Scott (D-GA): Not only is Ranking Member Scott the leading Democrat on House Ag, he is also a senior member of the House Financial Services Committee. At the FIT 21 markup, he said the bill has inadequate consumer protections, encourages vertical integration, and fails to sufficiently fund the CFTC. However, Rep. Scott signaled he was open to working with Chairman Thompson on improving the bill through amendments, as he concurred the status quo is not working to protect consumers. He also thanked Chairman Thompson for his work on the bill to date.

Rep. Yadira Caraveo (D-CO): Rep. Caraveo is the Ranking Member on the House Ag Commodity Markets, Digital Assets, and Rural Development. In comparison to her counterpart in the House Financial Services digital asset subcommittee, Rep. Stephen Lynch (D-MA), Rep. Caraveo has shown more willingness to work with Republicans on a market structure bill. At hearings, Rep. Caraveo has emphasized the importance of ensuring the CFTC has sufficient resources to enforce against fraud and manipulation in the crypto spot market, if given the authority. She has also voiced support for robust consumer protections and promoting financial inclusion in any crypto legislation.

Rep. Darren Soto (D-FL):  Rep. Soto is one of the most pro-crypto Members of Congress. Though not a sponsor of FIT 21, he has sponsored key crypto bills outside of House Ag’s jurisdiction. For example, he is a lead sponsor of the Blockchain Regulatory Certainty Act, a bill to clarify that blockchain developers and service providers who do not take control of customer assets are not subject to money service business licensing and registration requirements. He is also a cosponsor of the Keep Innovation In America Act to fix digital asset tax reporting requirements, and the Consumer Safety Technology Act, which directs the Department of Commerce and the Federal Trade Commission to study potential use cases of blockchain technology. Rep. Soto likely serves as a trusted resource on blockchain-related policy issues to fellow Democrat members on House Ag.

Closing Thoughts

In closing, next to House Financial Services, House Ag may be the most impactful House committee when it comes to crypto policy. As Chair Thompson and Subcommittee Chair Dusty Johnson are original sponsors of FIT 21, their efforts will be key to educating members about the bill and building broad support amongst the full House of Representatives. Further, while the committee will likely be focused on reauthorizing the farm bill next year, there will likely still be time for activity on crypto policy, particularly in the context of CFTC oversight.
