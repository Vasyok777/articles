Bittensor's Path Forward: Dynamic TAO and Smart Contracts

Seth Bloomberg

Oct 24, 2024 ⋅  11 min read

Key Insights

  • Bittensor, one of the leading AI x Crypto protocols, has two major network upgrades on the horizon: Dynamic TAO and EVM compatibility.
  • Dynamic TAO, which has been in development for most of this year, removes some of the network's centralization issues. Specifically, it will introduce a market-based mechanism for determining TAO emissions, whereas now emissions are generally determined by a handful of large TAO holders.
  • EVM compatibility will bring smart contract functionality to Bittensor, allowing developers to build onchain applications.
  • Together, these upgrades introduce new yield opportunities and, more broadly, change the risk environment for TAO holders and validators on the network.

The AI x Crypto vertical has exploded over the past year, driven and evidenced by the ~$370M in pre-seed and seed year-to-date funding. Despite the flurry of newly-funded projects pouring into the space, Bittensor continues to cement itself as a cornerstone piece of the AI x Crypto space.

However, the path forward for the protocol remains in flux. Two major upgrades, Dynamic TAO and EVM compatibility, will usher in a new era for Bittensor. Together, these releases shift Bittensor and its ecosystem away from its largely passive yield status quo and into an entirely new risk/reward environment. New yield opportunities will be available for risk-on TAO holders, and by the same token, new capital risks will be introduced into the system.

Below, we briefly discuss the network's current state and the impact these upcoming enhancements will have on the ecosystem and its core participants.

Bittensor Refresher

At Messari, we have extensively covered Bittensor. Below is a brief refresher on the network. For a comprehensive overview, refer to Sami Kassab’s seminal report on the protocol.

Bittensor can be viewed as an ecosystem of individual networks (subnets in Bittensor terminology), each focused on different AI objectives.

For example, subnet 9 is focused on AI model training, subnet 19 incentivizes model hosting and text inference services, and subnet 13 incentivizes large-scale data collection and storage.

Bittensor subnets have an owner who sets the objective and rules for the subnet. The miners on the subnet are like AI engineers, responsible for completing the task set by the subnet owner. A separate group of subnet validators runs the “validation stack,” which effectively tests and scores which miners perform best. Bittensor’s native token, TAO, is emitted to subnets, which is an incentive for completing the AI task set by the subnet owner.

Bittensor Centralization and Scalability Challenges

Like many new crypto networks, Bittensor’s early design and implementation have created centralization and scalability issues. These challenges can be framed as:

  • Centralization — The top 64 subnet validators decide the current allocation of TAO emissions to subnets. This centralized group of validators decides which subnets produce valuable commodities and distributes TAO emissions based on their subjective opinions.
  • Scalability — There are two scalability issues. One relates back to the TAO emission mechanism. Relying on a single set of validators (i.e., humans) to allocate emissions does not scale well, especially as subnets begin to number in the hundreds or possibly thousands. How can a single validator be expected to be knowledgeable and perform sufficient diligence across a growing number of subnets? The second scalability issue relates to miner expenses and revenues. As Sami Kassab states, there is no in-protocol mechanism to link miner demand to subnet usage. If a subnet finds traction and develops significant usage, miners risk seeing their expenses rise without an in-protocol means of scaling their revenues.

In the later section, Bucking the Bittensor Status Quo, we’ll discuss upcoming network upgrades that aim to resolve these challenges and the first and second-order effects they will introduce.

But first, we’ll explore the impact these issues and the overall network’s design have had on two primary Bittensor participants: validators and TAO holders.

Validator and TAO Holder Stasis

Bittensor’s design has caused participants in the network, specifically the validator set and TAO holders, to find somewhat of an activity equilibrium.

Validators generally aim to validate across every subnet to maximize their earnings, which helps them accumulate TAO delegations. Currently, the top five validators account for ~56% of the total staked TAO. The nature of validator incentives and TAO emissions are largely responsible for creating this equilibrium.

Subnet Validator Incentive Structure

Validators are rewarded for accumulating staked TAO and performing well across every subnet they validate. Within each subnet, the set of validators split 41% of the total TAO emitted to that subnet. The amount of TAO emissions an individual validator receives is a function of its TAO stake (the larger, the better) and how closely its ranking of miner performance matches the other validator's rankings of miners (i.e., how well they stay in consensus with other subnet validators).

Given this incentive structure, validators aim to maximize their staked TAO and successfully validate across each subnet to capture TAO emissions. In Bittensor’s current design, there is no capital at risk for validating on a subnet, meaning there is little downside (outside of uncovered validator operational expenses) in expanding into new subnets.

TAO Holder Incentive Structure

Currently, the primary utility of the TAO token from a retail holder perspective is the delegation system. Delegating to validators enables a TAO holder to capture some portion of the inflationary TAO emissions, conceptually similar to many Proof-of-Stake (PoS) networks. Validators set a take rate (usually between 9% and 18%, but can be set to 0%), which defines the TAO emission split between delegators and the validator. Taostats has built a simple tool to help holders see their projected returns based on which validator they delegate to. The staking APR depends on the validator but generally ranges between 10% and 14%.

So, from a TAO tokenholder perspective, this largely becomes a set-it-and-forget-it exercise. Find a validator with a good history of performant validation (effectively captured by its VTrust score) and a large amount of staked TAO relative to other validators and simply delegate to them. This is a low-effort strategy to nearly maximize the yield for a TAO holder.

Collectively, the above-described interactions between market participants (i.e., validators and TAO holders) and the associated market structure can be summarized as:

  1. Validators are strongly incentivized to continuously accumulate staked TAO and validate as many subnets as possible. Validators have no capital risk (i.e., no risk of losing their TAO principal) by validating on the marginal subnet.
  2. To minimize dilution, TAO holders must stake to the network (practically, this means delegating to a validator). The nature of TAO emissions incentivizes holders to delegate to validators with large TAO holdings and validation operations on many subnets.
  3. Both validators and TAO holders largely have no “in-protocol” means of expressing risk preferences. Staking TAO partially counteracts the network's natural inflation, but beyond staking, there is no in-protocol avenue to take on additional risk in exchange for (potentially) outsized returns.

Now, with the inception of Dynamic TAO and EVM compatibility on the horizon, new and novel forms of yield opportunities and capital allocation will be introduced to the network, nullifying all three of the above points.

Bucking the Bittensor Status Quo: Shifting to an Active Network

The progression of Bittensor from the above-described status quo to an active ecosystem will heavily impact validators and TAO holders. Both Dynamic TAO and EVM compatibility catalyze this transition.

Dynamic TAO

Dynamic TAO represents a paradigm shift regarding TAO economics and subnet architecture. We provide a brief overview of Dynamic TAO below; for a comprehensive overview of Dynamic TAO, please refer to Sami Kassab’s report.

In its current instantiation, validators on Bittensor’s system-level subnet, the Root Network, determine how TAO emissions are distributed to subnets. Dynamic TAO will effectively replace the function of the Root Network with a new market-based approach to determining the distribution of TAO emissions. With Dynamic TAO, each subnet will have its own token and a Uniswap V2 liquidity pool structure such that the subnet token is paired with TAO. Swapping TAO for the subnet token within the subnet liquidity pool will be the only means to acquire the subnet token.

To validate on a subnet, a validator must acquire a subnet token via delegation or staking TAO into the liquidity pool in exchange for some amount of the subnet token. The Uniswap V2 pool structure (i.e., the well-known x*y = k invariant) defines the price of a subnet token in terms of TAO. Higher-priced subnet tokens should, theoretically, indicate that the subnet is producing relatively more valuable outputs than other subnets. TAO emissions are then distributed to subnets based on their relative pricing against other subnet tokens.

Subnet tokenomics will closely mimic the TAO token, modeled after Bitcoin (e.g., 21 million capped supply, no pre-mine, TAO emitted each block with a built-in halving schedule).

Instead of the 21 million supply cap, subnet tokens will have a total supply of 42 million, with half distributed to the subnet liquidity pool and the other half distributed as an incentive to miners and validators.

Dynamic TAO Market and Yield Impact

The first-order impact of Dynamic TAO is that it solves the scalability and centralized decision-making issues around TAO emissions. With Dynamic TAO and subnet liquidity pools, any TAO holder (i.e., the broader market) gets to signal, via staking and swapping into the subnet token, that a particular subnet is valuable and deserving of emissions.

However, market participants must be aware that capital is now at risk. For example, choosing to stake TAO to a particular subnet effectively means swapping TAO for that subnet token. Validators will then be forced to evaluate the subnets they participate in closely, leading to more efficient emission allocation relative to the current model.

Given the Uniswap V2 liquidity pool mechanism, the exchange rate of TAO to subnet token will change over time. If more TAO flows into a liquidity pool (signifying demand for the subnet token) than its subnet token counterpart, then the price of the subnet token (in TAO terms) increases. The reverse, of course, is true if TAO liquidity flows out of a subnet pool.

The subnet liquidity pool design, coupled with TAO and subnet token emissions, enables the risk expressivity that has largely been missing within the Bittensor ecosystem. While some of the Dynamic TAO details remain unfinalized, it should largely hold that early holders of successful subnets will see significant upside (denominated in TAO returns). Early holders can accumulate relatively more of the subnet token supply since it follows the Bitcoin emission model, and half of its supply is distributed to miners and validators. If the subnet gains traction and is perceived to produce valuable outputs, it should attract more TAO inflows and, thus, more TAO emissions, leading to deeper liquidity within the subnet liquidity pools. Subnet token holders can then leverage a deeply liquid subnet pool to rebalance their portfolio with minimal slippage.

Risking capital cuts both ways. Due to the nature of Uniswap V2 liquidity pools, swapping into a subnet token implies the risk of losing a small to significant portion of previously held TAO. Allocators of TAO now must be subnet-context aware to maximize their position or, on the other hand, minimize their losses.

In any case, the yield landscape will dramatically expand once Dynamic TAO goes live.

EVM Compatibility

Since Bittensor is a substrate-based blockchain, it can leverage existing code and packages to create EVM compatibility. From an architectural perspective, the EVM compatibility code is effectively additional software Bittensor nodes will run. End users and application developers will have a familiar EVM experience when interacting with applications and developer tooling. Moonbeam, for example, is another substrate-based chain with EVM compatibility.

Smart Contract Capabilities on Bittensor

With EVM compatibility, Bittensor evolves from what could loosely be considered a network of “appchains” with a singular mission to a more generalized smart contract platform.

Smart contracts and onchain applications mean more utility and demand for the TAO token. As TAO is the native token of the chain, it’s the likely candidate to be used as collateral, for example, within DeFi protocols, as well as the gas token for transacting. From an onchain yield perspective, this creates new opportunities for TAO holders.

In addition to DeFi protocols and other verticals that most smart contract platforms eventually cultivate, smart contracts capabilities on Bittensor enable more granular, onchain interactions between validators, miners, and subnet owners. For example, validators and miners could make onchain revenue share agreements (via smart contracts) based on specific key performance metrics being achieved (e.g., a specific miner’s model reaching some specified high level of onchain usage).

Final Thoughts

Bittensor is on the precipice of a network-wide paradigm shift. Dynamic TAO and smart contract functionality fundamentally change the risk/reward calculation of Bittensor market participants and simultaneously create a new yield meta within the ecosystem.

The success of Bittensor now largely hinges on the market appetite for risk-on behavior regarding subnets and their associated tokens and whether smart contract functionality can attract a new class of Bittensor builders — application developers.
