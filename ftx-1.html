The Week in Crypto Policy: Decentralization & Policy Considerations
George Leonardo

Nov 3, 2023   10 min read

This week, we asked Rashan Colbert, Head of Policy at dYdX, to help break down DeFi and key considerations for policymakers.

dYdX Interview: Decentralization & Policy


From the IRS's recently proposed tax rules for digital asset "brokers" to legislative efforts to combat illicit finance, policymakers are grappling with how to regulate decentralized networks, particularly when it comes to decentralized finance ("DeFi").

While laws written for traditional finance presume a heavy reliance on centralized intermediaries, DeFi's fundamental value proposition is that it reduces or eliminates the very need to rely on - or trust - a central intermediary (e.g., an FTX). As such, DeFi raises novel and unique challenges for Members of Congress and regulators.

The interview below aims to shed more light on DeFi and key related policy concerns.

It has been edited for formatting purposes only (e.g., certain portions are bolded for emphasis).

Interviewee Background

dYdX is a decentralized exchange that allows users to trade crypto assets without relying on an intermediary.

Rashan Colbert is the Head of Policy at dYdX Trading Inc. Previously, he worked on Capitol Hill for Senator Cory Booker (D-NJ).

Interview

Q1: How would you explain decentralized finance to someone who is new to crypto and blockchain technology?

Answer:

Its important to first understand the concept of the blockchain, which originates from the 2008 whitepaper describing Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System, but has since been modified for uses beyond cryptocurrency. Blockchain is a decentralized technology that uses a distributed ledger to transparently record transactions and store data. Users of a blockchain must verify each transaction independently before it is confirmed - meaning no central authority is making decisions on behalf of all users.

Decentralized Finance, or DeFi, uses blockchain technology to automate financial services on the web, reducing or eliminating the need for traditional intermediaries like banks or centralized exchanges to verify transactions. Transactions utilizing true DeFi protocols are completely transparent, removing the black boxes of trust required in the traditional financial system and reducing the vulnerable or private information users typically have to give away in order to transact.

Decentralized Finance exists within an ecosystem of DeFi protocols that are accessible and auditable via the Internet. A protocol is a set of rules, processes, or procedures for transmitting data between computers. Some protocols your readers might be familiar with are TCP/IP (the Internet protocol suite), SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol, i.e. email), or HTTP (used to load webpages using hypertext links).

Q2: What is dYdX, and what is the fundamental problem that it's trying to solve?

Answer:

dYdX is a leading DeFi exchange protocol that currently supports the trading of perpetual futures contracts in permitted jurisdictions. The mission is to democratize access to financial opportunity. We do this by building a powerful and professional exchange with the benefits of security, privacy, and decentralization.

A perpetual contract is a type of derivative product that gets its value from an underlying asset, in our case: a digital asset like Bitcoin. A derivative enables traders to have exposure to an assets price movements without being required to actually hold the underlying asset. Traders may use derivatives to mitigate losses (hedging) or take on risk with the expectation they will profit (speculating).

Perpetual futures, as the name suggests, revolve around the future value of a given asset. In traditional finance, futures contracts are dated: meaning they are set to expire on an agreed-upon date. Perpetual futures contracts remain open indefinitely and must be closed manually. Since perpetual contracts never expire, there are unique mechanisms in regards to margining and a funding rate that affect their pricing.

Q3: Could you explain the relationship between dYdX Trading the company and dYdX the protocol? In particular, to what extent is the protocol itself decentralized?

Answer:

dYdX Trading is the U.S.-based, New York City headquartered company that I work for. We are professionals who develop software and support the dYdX protocol. In previous versions of the protocol, dYdX Trading operated some offchain features including a matching engine and order book, making dYdX a hybrid decentralized exchange.

For about two and a half years dYdX has functioned as a Layer-2 protocol sitting atop the Ethereum network using smart contracts developed by a third party. Trades settle on the Layer-2 system, which publishes zero-knowledge proofs periodically to an Ethereum smart contract in order to prove that state transitions within Layer 2 are valid.

As of last month, dYdX has released open-source software that would allow third parties to utilize Layer 1 application chains. This will be called the dYdX Chain. The dYdX Chain is a standalone, open-source, proof-of-stake blockchain with a fully decentralized order book and matching engine capable of scaling to orders of magnitude more throughput than otherwise possible.

This new version of the dYdX protocol is fully decentralized with each validator running its own orderbook with orders and cancellations propagated throughout the network. dYdX Trading Inc. does not control any part of the dYdX Chain - it purely develops and open-sources software that others can utilize.

Q4: What are some of the advantages of having a decentralized trading platform controlled by a community, as opposed to one central intermediary?

Answer:

Decentralization is core to the dYdX mission to democratize access to financial opportunity. We believe decentralization will drive radical improvements in transparency, safety, fairness, and equality of opportunity.

For the first time, with DeFi, the financial system doesnt have to be a black box to everyday people. Users can trust, or choose not to trust, open-source, fully auditable code instead of having to trust corporations.

Community control of a trading platform brings a significant increase in fairness, leveling of opportunity, and alignment of goals and incentives.

Q5: Conversely, what are some of the risks a decentralized trading platform poses to individual users, and how should U.S. policymakers address those risks?

Answer:

Decentralized networks empower individuals to take control over their own assets and balance accessibility and security trade-offs as they see fit. Crypto enables users to individually hold onto their own property in a cash-like capacity not possible with traditional assets.

Not your keys, not your coins is a common refrain heard in crypto circles that encourages users to manage their own assets rather than leave them in the custody of centralized entities, like some exchanges, who users must trust to have their best interests in mind. This idea requires an increased level of responsibility from users than is asked from them by more traditional institutions like banks, who manage assets for them.

Decentralization comes with a particular set of benefits and trade-offs that should be adequately considered when interacting with decentralized networks, or when crafting policies that will govern them.

Q7: Policymakers, particularly in the Senate, are concerned that DeFi can, and has been, exploited by bad actors for illicit purposes. For example, the CANSEE Act aims to address illicit finance concerns by applying existing sanction laws and anti-money laundering laws that apply to traditional financial institutions to certain DeFi developers and investors.

Is the CANSEE Act approach workable? If not, how should policymakers address illicit finance concerns while accounting for the unique characteristics of DeFi?

Answer:

There are a number of pieces of legislation focused on crypto and illicit finance floating around Congress right now - you actually did a good job summarizing them in a recent issue! Theres CANSEE, the Crypto-Asset National Security Enhancement and Enforcement Act, from the group of four national security-focused senators, and there are a few others.

Each of these bills comes from a reasonable place: a desire to close the regulatory gaps where they might exist in order to prevent dangerous practices by bad actors. This is a goal that I, and many others in DeFi, agree with and that I think is possible to find the right answers with a bit more study and collaboration.

What we must avoid is the creation of policies that will only result in pushing crypto development offshore, perpetuating an unregulated market where illicit finance can flourish beyond the easy reach of the US federal government. Enactment of the wrong policy can unintentionally incentivize bad actors abroad while discouraging good innovators at home.

Public, transparent blockchains have inherent properties that make them fantastic tools for law enforcement to track the movement of illicit money. Good policy will leverage these traits for our shared ends, and maintain good oversight of on and off ramps.

Q8: What are some policy issues that you would like to see Congress address this year or next and why? Are there any specific pieces of legislation that youd like to see enacted?

Answer:

Its time for stablecoin legislation to be passed. Dollar-denominated stablecoins are at the core of the crypto ecosystem and are rapidly finding adoption throughout the global economy. To that end, I think the Clarity for Payment Stablecoins Act, passed through the House Financial Services Committee this summer, is a good vehicle to work with. I know that there are still issues held by the Administration and Democratic committee leadership, but I think the effort to work through them now, to see this bill through, would be worth it.


Q9: What advice would you give individuals who are looking to advocate for policies that protect and promote DeFi innovation in the U.S.? In other words, how can they make the biggest impact with policymakers and their staffs?

Answer:

Hot take #1: Crypto Twitter is not the place where policy is made.

Hot take #2: Your voice really does matter in American democracy.

It is a great thing that so many of us have access to public platforms where we can broadcast our opinions for all to see. In fact, I actually think its important for people to share their perspectives to keep the public conversation alive. But it is not where real decisions are being made. Twitter activism can inform the conversation, but its important to go further.

Some of the most incredible moments of democracy in action I saw during my seven years on Capitol Hill came from regular people making their voices heard. Votes that we knew were going to go one way were changed because constituents decided to speak up. Taking the time to look up the information of your representative and your senators, picking up the phone, and delivering a message about important issues really matters. Sensible DeFi policy is going to remain an uphill battle until everyday Americans make their voices heard.

Closing: Rashan, thank you so much for taking the time to answer these questions.

Rashan: My pleasure. Thanks for the opportunity!

Quick Hits

  • The Government Accountability Office concluded that the SEC didn't follow the Congressional Review Act when issuing Staff Accounting Bulletin 121. Congress can overturn "SAB 121" with a simple majority vote in the House and Senate and with President Biden's signature (or veto and two-thirds majority vote in House and Senate).
    • Further, if a court were to agree with GAO's finding that SAB 121 is a "rule" under the APA, it could set aside SAB 121 because the SEC did not follow the APA's notice and comment procedural requirements when issuing the bulletin. See 5 U.S.C. 706.
      • GAO Report.
  • Rep. Sean Casten (D-IL) plans to introduce companion legislation to Senator Elizabeth Warren's Digital Asset Anti-Money Laundering Act in the coming weeks.
    • His Op-Ed in the Hill: "Stopping future crypto-financed terrorism."
  • Senator Ron Wyden (D-OR), Senate Finance Chairman, sent a letter to the IRS asking how proposed budget cuts would impact the agency's ability to address financial crimes, including ones involving the use of crypto.
  • SEC subpoenas PayPal over PayPal USD Stablecoin.
