FTX Meltdown: Implications and Insights
Stephanie Dunbar
Kel
Kunal Goel

Nov 10, 2022 ⋅  10 min read

Key Insights

  • In a throwback to the early days of crypto, FTX, the third largest crypto exchange, halted user withdrawals during a bank run. There is reportedly a shortfall of $8 billion.
  • Sam Bankman-Fried, the founder of the exchange, was the poster boy for the crypto industry’s efforts to find legitimacy. In a twist of irony, his blow up and alleged mismanagement may set the industry back again in the eyes of regulators.
  • FTX and Alameda Research were prominent supporters of Solana and its ecosystem. SOL is now down 50% over the past two days as supporters try to rebuild amid the uncertainty.

Background on the Drama

What. A. Week. The world’s third largest crypto exchange just went belly up, and the industry’s white knight got dragged down in the process. Grab a (Irish) coffee, and let’s figure out what the hell happened and what the implications are.

Sam Bankman-Fried (SBF) is the CEO of FTX, one of the world’s largest exchanges and also principal shareholder of Alameda Research, a separate trading firm he founded prior. There is also an entity called FTX.US. FTX.US is operated as a distinct U.S. operation, separate from FTX with SBF again acting as a principal though not overseeing day-to-day operations. FTX has received funding from big names like BlackRock, SoftBank, Temasek, Tiger Global, and the Ontario Teachers' Pension Plan. FTX was also a large investor themselves through FTX Ventures, making over 60 individual investments. The full list of investors can be found on our Fundraising page.

FTX has an exchange token called FTT which can be used for benefits on FTX like lower fees and cheaper withdrawals. It is also considered pseudo-equity as a third of the revenue generated on the exchange is used to buyback and burn FTT, tying its price to the success of FTX.

The relationship between FTX and Alameda was always a bit unclear for outside parties, as SBF obviously played a pivotal role in the direction of the two distinct entities.

The trouble began when Coindesk reported on November 2 that Alameda's assets (~$6 billion of the ~$15 billion) were in FTX's FTT token. This amount of balance sheet concentration, the fact that it was largely illiquid (representing ~2x the circulating supply of FTT), and the link between the two entities caused immediate market concern. SBF made the link even more dubious by promoting the token on Twitter as recently as Halloween.

On November 6, Binance CEO Changpeng “CZ” Zhao announced that his exchange would liquidate $2.1 billion worth of FTT. Alameda offered to buy it at the market price of $22. CZ publicly seemed to decline…and so did FTT. Sharply.

With the price of FTT rapidly declining, concerns arose about FTX’s liquidity and whether user funds were backed. About $6 billion was withdrawn in 72 hours before withdrawals were halted on the afternoon of November 8. Later that day Binance agreed, in principle, to acquire FTX and stem any liquidity concerns. However, after reviewing the FTX balance sheet, they have since walked away from the deal citing “the issues are beyond our control or ability to help.” They also explicitly cited “reports regarding mishandled customer funds and alleged U.S. agency investigations” as other reasons.

Just this morning (November 10) SBF released a statement blaming an internal error of tracking user margins for the liquidity crunch. He said FTX would spend the week trying to raise liquidity from investors and all funds will hopefully be returned to users. We’ll see.

So where does this leave us?

Broad Implications and Unknown Contagions (Stephanie Dunbar)

FTX's blow up is arguably the largest event in crypto history given the size of the exchange and the presence of SBF and FTX in DC and mainstream culture. About $8 billion in user liquidity is apparently at risk, and faith is deteriorating rapidly in tokens associated with FTX. FTX is a large supporter of the Solana ecosystem. SOL was down ~50%, and SRM down ~60% between November 7 and 9 alone.

VCs and large crypto institutions like Jump, Paradigm, and Multicoin are rumored to be hurting due to significant equity in FTX, holdings in FTT and Solana ecosystem tokens, and funds stuck on the exchange. FTX’s latest funding round in January 2022 injected $400 million into the company, valuing it at $32 billion. All equity in FTX may now be obsolete.

The global crypto market cap was down 11% between November 7 and 9 as the entire crypto market suffers in FTX's wake. Being one of the largest crypto exchanges in the world, and one of the most popular in the US (though the exact fate of FTX.US is still unknown), the ripple effect of entities and individuals that will be burned will be widespread, especially if no bail-out event is to occur.

Further, regulators and policymakers (some that already want crypto on a stake) that once listened to Sam’s lobbying efforts may now have serious doubts, or ammunition. While all of the details are yet to surface, crypto’s reputation will inevitably suffer. Newer entrants will lose faith and leave the space, and new investment interest will be much harder to come by. Less capital inflows means less incentives for building. Hurting investors and a diminishing industry reputation could take us deeper into the bear than originally imagined.

Solana Caught in the Crosshairs (Kunal Goel)

FTX and Alameda were key supporters of Solana and its ecosystem :

  • They were investors in multiple rounds of Solana holding at least $1.2 billion worth of SOL as of June 30, 2022.
  • They run a central limit order book exchange on Solana called Serum. Serum is ubiquitous across the Solana ecosystem being utilized by other applications such as Raydium (an AMM), Jet Protocol (a lending market), Oxygen (a prime brokerage), and more.
  • They were investors in many projects across the ecosystem and often led investment rounds. They had investments in lifestyle app STEPN, lending market Solend, block explorer Solscan, and stablecoin protocol UXD, among others.
  • They were also involved in other capacities, as well, like running an NFT marketplace.

The fall from grace of one of its greatest supporters has affected the value of the Solana ecosystem materially. There are open questions as to how the tokens that are vesting to Alameda Research will be handled.

Some founders have discussed the possibility of jumping ship and saving their project, but they have not found supporters in their communities, as might be expected. As such, tweets have been deleted, but there is evidence of at least one prominent NFT project considering such a move.

Another key risk is the threat of large amounts of Alameda’s investment in SOL being unstaked to be sold. Currently, 34 million SOL is in queue to be unstaked at the end of the current epoch, estimated to occur around 0830 UTC on November 10, 2022. With Solana’s falling price, lowering stake and lower confidence among builders, there may be a chance of a flywheel of worsening fundamentals and price. Lower demand through fewer applications and use cases and lower security through lower price and staked supply can further discourage investors and developers, leading to a further worsening of price, stake, and fundamentals.

The protocol parameters allow up to 25% of absolute change in stake per epoch. The net amount queue to be unstaked this coming epoch currently stands at a reasonably comfortable 7%, with a capacity for 73 million more SOL to be unstaked.

Having said that, we should note that culture, community, and the Lindy effect are important, especially after the events of the week. If Solana emerges from this, it will finally have culture outside of Sam and FTX, and the experience of overcoming an external black swan event. It will have builders that are committed to the ecosystem, not grifters that are building under five different aliases. Its holder community will become hardened believers. Concentration of holdings has always been a common criticism of Solana considering its fundraising history, but a large distribution event may occur as a result of this downfall, leading to a more equitable distribution. Solana will grow and learn from its mistake of having too many eggs in one basket. If it emerges, it will no longer be a “VC chain,” the stake will become more decentralized and a new Sun will rise.

There is Still Hope for Solana (Kel Eleje)

To present a possible long-term positive case for Solana, we may draw parallels to a major historical event: the liquidation of ETH held in ICO treasuries back in 2018.

In 2018, serious sell pressure on ETH cast doubt on the longevity of the Ethereum ecosystem. Teams raced to sell ETH for expenses they originally thought they’d be able to cover with inflated token valuations. Most teams went to zero.

The fate of Solana could play out the same way. There may be massive sell pressure still to come as a result of FTX’s implosion. And 99% of teams may get wiped out. But we cannot ignore the 1% of teams building legitimate DeFi, dev tooling, gaming, and consumer applications on Solana.

Among them may well be the next SNX, the next AAVE (or LEND as it was back then), the next Punks, or the next Axie. All of those projects raised funds or launched on Ethereum in 2018 when public sentiment was certain that ETH and zero would soon meet. Today, impending SOL unlocks have placed sentiment on Solana in a similar spot.

We have no crystal ball, but Solana has legitimate, seemingly non-fraudulent projects worth watching across several sectors, including:

  • DeFi: projects like Marinade Finance, UXD Protocol, Drift Protocol, Zeta Markets, and Friktion Labs;
  • Infra: projects like Render Network, Helium Labs, Jito Labs, and Genesys Go;
  • Gaming: projects like Aurory, Star Atlas, BR1: Infinite.

If these projects are worth their salt, now is the time to look at them more closely — when the whole world is counting them out. Should any of the legitimate projects on Solana succeed in the long run, the sun shall rise again on the chain someday.

Don’t just take our word for it though. Investors with a strong track record are ready to place their bets on the Solana ecosystem for the long term.

Wrapping Up

While we certainly thought it couldn't get worse after Terra, Three Arrows Capital, or Celsius, we found out this week that unfortunately, it could. The “effective altruist” who branded himself as legitimizing the crypto industry in the eyes of regulators, turned out to be flawed and fallible in the end.

All is not lost, though. While the market bleeds, the die-hards stay to build out yet another day. Blocks are still being proposed and mined. Not all will survive, but the philosophies of self-custody and verifiability that built the crypto industry remain alive.Before the industry can thrive and bring decentralization to the mainstream, the rent seekers have to be purged. Let’s just hope this was the last one.
