Economic Security and its Memes
Kunal Goel
Peter Horton

Jun 14, 2024   8 min read

This is not an Ethereum versus Solana debate. But Ethereum supporters typically tend to fall into the camp that economic security matters while Solana supporters do not. So, like most things, this is a bit of an Ethereum versus Solana debate.

Primer

Cryptoeconomic security is a foundational idea in blockchain design where token incentives help coordinate selfishly motivated entities to create a stable game-theoretic outcome. Bitcoin pioneered this through its Proof-of-Work consensus algorithm, where miners solve cryptographic puzzles for BTC rewards which secures the blockchain from 51% attacks. Research by Amina Group on mining parity empirically establishes that mining hash rate (security) and price are directly linked in Proof-of-Work blockchains.

In Proof-of-Stake (PoS) blockchains, the behavior of validators is similarly aligned towards a common good through token rewards (carrot) and slashing (stick). In Ethereum, proposing and voting on valid blocks generates rewards, whereas missing votes or double voting incurs a penalty. Some PoS blockchains forego slashing as a disincentive, instead relying on missed rewards as the penalty.

However, the revolution in smart contracts has created out-of-protocol incentives for validators. These opportunities may be clubbed under the broad umbrella of maximally extractable value (MEV). At typical levels of stake distribution, validators earn MEV for one block at a time, which is usually not high enough to justify an attack. However, if a malicious validator gains control of more than 50% of the stake, they can handpick which transactions are processed in perpetuity and thereby generate significant MEV. Therefore, a networks staked capital is typically considered its economic security as an offending validator will have to acquire and risk a portion of this value to fully take over block production. The primary argument against economic security is that in case of such an attack, the social layer, i.e., the honest users, will coordinate to remove the offending validator and slash their stake.

In this report, our analysts discuss the following ideas:

  1. What is economic security, and is it all just a meme?
  2. Is economic security useful to applications and developers? Multicoins Kyle Samani commented that none of its portfolio companies has ever worried about whether their project has enough cryptoeconomic security.
  3. Is economic security useful for users? Are there any risks if a user holds $1 billion in stablecoins on a chain with $100 in total staked capital?
  4. Is economic security useful in comparing blockchains? Does demand for security drive value?

Kunal Goel

All Models Are Wrong


While I cant formally prove that there is a relationship between demand for security and its supply in a Proof-of-Stake system, there is empirical evidence. I also know that if I had $1 billion USDC, I would not hold it on a PoS chain with $100 in staked capital. I would be concerned that anyone with $51 could take over its block production and prevent me from redeeming my assets. While I could perhaps redeem it through litigation and slashing, it would still be an unnecessary risk. Instead, I would either move my USDC offchain or buy the native asset for sufficient economic security. With this intuitive understanding supported by empirical evidence, I posit that demand for security is one of the key drivers for the value of a native asset. In my reports, I propose quantitative expressions for demand for security and discuss why it may be superior to other commonly used metrics such as total value locked, MEV, users, transactions, etc.

While there may not have been attacks on PoS networks so far, if blockchains are to secure trillions of dollars worth of assets in the future, high economic security may be helpful for smooth operations. For developers, understanding the value drivers of blockchains helps them allocate resources more efficiently and utilize solutions like EigenLayer that provide flexible economic security.

Having said that, I do concede that all criticisms of cryptoeconomic security are somewhat valid to differing extents. Ill try to address why economic security is relevant despite its criticisms.

Recovery through the Social Layer

As blockchains decentralize, the social layer gets extremely expensive and hard to coordinate. There may be thousands of validators and hundreds of thousands of stakeholders that must agree on how and to which block to roll back the chain. There may even be differing opinions on which block to roll back the chain to. For example, if Circle redeems $1 billion in USDC and it is later discovered that an attacker had started exploiting AMM pools on the same block, Circle may push for a rollback to a point after the transfer was made onchain. A similar situation may happen with a bridge transferring irrecoverable assets on a different chain against assets on the attacked chain. Trying to coordinate the social layer will continue to become more problematic and contentious as blockchain adoption grows and the value transacted on them increases.

Therefore, it is useful to have high economic disincentives against such an attack. In fact, using the above example, this value should scale with the amount of Real-World Assets or average bridged assets onchain. The Centralized Stablecoin Multiple uses the sum of centralized stablecoins, the largest RWAs, as an indicator for cryptoeconomic security demand.

Legal Recourse

Trustlessness and permissionlessness are key features of decentralized blockchains. Any user in any regulatory jurisdiction should be able to join block production. Trusting that there will be legal recourse somewhat defeats the purpose of these decentralized systems as that feature is available in centralized systems too. Additionally, some jurisdictions may not even consider it illegal to control block production, as it is a feature of controlling 50%+ stake.

Other Attack Vectors

In a delegated Proof-of-Stake or liquid staking system, there may be easier ways to acquire 51% stake by coercing a handful of large validators. For example, Solana has 1,500 validators, but only 41 control 51% of the stake. Compromising 41 validators may be easier than acquiring $20+ billion in SOL tokens.

There may be other vectors of attack too, such as:

  • If there is concentrated holding, an attacker may be able to coerce stake delegation to themselves without incurring the economic cost.
  • An attacker may exploit a bug in the code.
  • An attacker may denial-of-service attack the chain by spamming transactions.

All these attack vectors exist; however, cryptoeconomic security deals with economic attacks. A strong password is still useful even if it is vulnerable to a $5 wrench attack. All blockchains should attempt to minimize other attack vectors through distributed validator sets, multiple node implementations, distributed holding, proper transaction fees, etc.

Peter Horton

I think everyone agrees that theres some middle ground between economic security is completely worthless and the security of a network is equal to its economic security. But if picking a side, I fall closer to the formers camp: that the economic security (the staked market cap of a network or some threshold of it) is overrated to a networks overall security.

As Anatoly Yakovenko argues, this is mainly because the operational cost to attack a network is upper-bounded by, but does not equal, its economic security. It didnt cost Lido $33 billion to obtain 28% of Ethereums economic security. In DPoS networks, the same logic applies for any validator with delegated stake.

Thus, economic security is important to the extent that a malicious actor could purchase 33% of stake and cause a liveness failure. But its irrelevant from the standpoint that there are many cheaper ways for a malicious actor to obtain 33% of stake.

This is all assuming that there are honest nodes who detect and fork out the malicious actor. So whats ultimately most important to a networks security is the number of physical nodes (validating and non-validating) and related factors such as:

  • The number of entities running those nodes
  • The distribution of stake among validators (the Nakamoto coefficient) and across various dimensions, such as hosting locations, hosting providers, validator clients, and delegators

These factors were all cited by Visa when explaining why it chose Solana for its stablecoin settlement pilot expansion. Economic security was nowhere to be found. Similarly, Render mentioned Solanas validator count and Nakamoto coefficient (but nothing about its economic security) during its expansion. As noted above, Kyle Samani stated that none of Multicoins portcos have shared concerns about a networks economic security.

While these metrics are more important, theyre tougher to source and display as a clean figure compared to the staked market cap, hence the industrys overreliance on economic security. If youre interested in a deep dive into these metrics, check out our report Evaluation Validator Decentralization: Geographic and Infrastructure Distribution in Proof-of-Stake Networks.

Even if the cost to attack a network was equal to its economic security, there are still other considerations when speaking practically. For example, if youre holding USDC, how much do you care about the economic security knowing that Circle is running its own node? As Kunal notes, an attacker could cause liveness failures which would be inconvenient to you, but they have no viable path to steal your funds. So maybe you want some reasonable minimum of staked market cap but does $100 million vs. $5 billion really matter?

Furthermore, when discussing the current validator landscape, its important to note that a common set of doxxed entities combine for a large share of stake across many networks. By acting maliciously, these entities would face legal and reputational risk.

To take Kunals example, would I rather hold $1 billion USDC on a network with $1 billion staked compared to $100 staked? Yes. But what if the network with $1 billion staked is run by 100 undoxxed validators with no Circle full node support whereas the network with $100 staked is run by 10,000 doxxed validators, operating in recognized states, with Circle full node support? Ill take the latter. So lets stop reducing network security to its economic security.
