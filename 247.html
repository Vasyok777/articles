The Week in Crypto Policy: Tokenization - Robert Leshner, Superstate, Interview

George Leonardo

Oct 25, 2024 ⋅  7 min read

Intro

Robert Leshner is the CEO and Co-Founder of Superstate, an asset management firm specializing in modernizing investing through tokenized financial products. Previously, he co-founded Compound Labs in 2017, which developed Compound — one of the first decentralized finance applications to allow users to borrow cryptocurrency or earn interest on their crypto holdings.

The Q&A below is aimed at highlighting the intersection of entrepreneurship, tokenization, and policy.

Q & A

The Crypto Entrepreneur

Q1: What led you to start your first business, and why did you choose to build in crypto?

A1: I entered the crypto space in 2017 by co-founding Compound Labs as a way to start addressing inefficiencies in financial markets — where transactions are slow, opaque, and reliant on intermediaries. Blockchain offers a more transparent, automated, and efficient way to reimagine these systems.

This technology reduces friction, broadens market access, and fosters innovation that benefits the economy. Its transparency and programmability enable better oversight, lower costs, and more inclusive markets. This potential now drives my work at Superstate, where we focus on building financial investing products that promote transparency, efficiency, and stability.

Q2: What do you think is the biggest challenge facing crypto entrepreneurs in the U.S. today?

A2: The biggest challenge is the lack of clear, modern regulations. While many countries are advancing frameworks that encourage innovation, the U.S. has fallen behind, relying on outdated laws not designed for digital assets. This regulatory uncertainty creates a vacuum, leading to inconsistent enforcement and making it difficult for entrepreneurs to build confidently. Without clear guidelines, the U.S. risks stifling growth and losing its leadership in financial innovation.

Superstate & Tokenization

Q3: For those who may be unfamiliar with the concept of real-world-asset tokenization, could you explain what it means? Relatedly, what would you say are the key benefits of having assets represented onchain?

A3: Tokenization is the process of recording ownership on a blockchain instead of, or alongside, traditional systems like spreadsheets or paper contracts. In simple terms, it replaces outdated infrastructure with digital tokens, streamlining the entire transaction process. This makes it faster, more cost-effective, and easier to manage.

The key benefits of having assets represented onchain include increased transparency, since every transaction is recorded on a public ledger, fostering trust and accountability. It also improves transferability, allowing assets to move efficiently between parties without delays or high costs. Additionally, tokenization enables automation through smart contracts, which execute transactions based on predefined rules, reducing the need for manual oversight. Together, these features make blockchain a powerful tool for modernizing financial markets.

Q4: Could you explain some of the products you offer at Superstate and who your main customers are?

A4: Superstate offers two tokenized financial products issued on the Ethereum blockchain: The first is USTB, a short-duration government bond fund that provides exposure to Treasury Bills. The second is USCC, a cryptoasset-based yield fund that uses market-neutral strategies to generate returns while minimizing volatility.

Our customers are qualified purchasers—primarily crypto-native hedge funds, venture funds, and traders. These firms are increasingly seeking exposure to traditional financial products, like Treasury Bills, but want to access them through the digital asset infrastructure they already operate within. Traditionally, moving between crypto markets and traditional financial markets can be cumbersome, requiring multiple intermediaries, transfers, and conversions. Our products solve this problem by allowing customers to hold and manage these assets directly onchain, reducing friction and operational complexity. This seamless access helps them manage liquidity and optimize returns without leaving the digital asset ecosystem.

Q5: How do you see the market for tokenized real-world assets evolving over the next 5-10 years? For instance, which assets do you think are most likely to be tokenized in the near future?

A5: We are still in the early stages of tokenization development, with tokenized short-term government debt emerging as an initial focus due to the size, liquidity, and stability of the market. While Treasury Bills are just the starting point, there is still significant room for growth in this area, especially as more institutions adopt tokenized infrastructure for managing cash and liquidity.

As the technology matures, we expect to see other fixed income strategies, such as private credit, follow suit. These markets, though less liquid, stand to benefit from the transparency, efficiency, and automation that tokenization can provide. Over the long term, tokenization has the potential to transform financial markets by reducing operational friction, improving access, and enhancing market oversight.

Policy

Q6: What would you say are the main regulatory roadblocks, if any, impeding the progress of tokenization today and how do you think they should be addressed?

A6: I agree with Chair Gensler’s principle of “same activity, same risk, same regulation,” but the reverse must also hold—new, tailored rules are necessary for novel activities. Currently, regulators are applying rules designed for an intermediated, analog system, which limits the potential of blockchain technology.

Following this year’s election, we are hopeful for a regulatory reset. A comprehensive review of issuer disclosures for tokenized securities would be a key step, along with a functional review of risks unique to activities like peer-to-peer or protocol-based trading. Issuers should disclose key details, such as how smart contracts function and the blockchain network used, as this information is essential for transparency and investor protection. However, legacy requirements—like offchain transfer agents—should be reconsidered, as they create unnecessary costs where the blockchain already serves this function.

The SEC has adapted to technological change before—when electronic trading replaced physical exchanges, it applied principles that balanced innovation with oversight. Similarly, tokenization and disintermediation offer new utility and choice for investors; regulators should embrace this shift. We remain committed to engaging with regulators and are optimistic that a change in leadership at the SEC will create room for progress.

Q7: Are there any specific pieces of legislation that you would like to see enacted in the near-future that are either directly or indirectly related to tokenization?

A7: The House Financial Services Committee, particularly the Subcommittee on Digital Assets, Financial Technology, and Inclusion, has done commendable work in advancing education and public discourse around tokenization and DeFi. Chair McHenry has been a strong advocate for digital assets, and while his retirement is a loss, members like French Hill, Andy Barr, Warren Davidson, and Tom Emmer have been deeply engaged with these issues. Additionally, Ranking Member Maxine Waters recently acknowledged that “crypto is inevitable,” and Ritchie Torres has been vocal in challenging the SEC’s broad interpretation of securities laws.

Looking ahead, we hope to see stablecoin legislation passed during the lame-duck session. This would reduce the state-by-state compliance burden and provide much-needed clarity for digital dollars within the digital asset ecosystem. Bipartisan support appears to be building, making this a practical step toward establishing clearer regulatory guidelines for tokenization.

Q8: As Superstate products are currently only offered to Qualified Purchasers, do you think there are ways the U.S. could expand investment opportunities for individuals and families who don’t meet the current requirements for Qualified Purchaser and/or Accredited Investor status? For example, are there ways to balance wider access to investment opportunities with maintaining appropriate investor protections?

A8: We would love to make our tokenized securities available to more investors, as we believe these products offer low-risk exposure with additional utility beyond what traditional securities can provide. Broader access would empower individuals and families to benefit from the same opportunities available to institutional investors, helping to modernize their approach to savings and investment.

However, expanding access under the current regulatory framework poses challenges. To register our products and make them available for retail investors, we would need to limit the utility of our tokenized funds, which defeats the purpose of tokenization. Our products are designed to be transferable peer-to-peer, used for in-kind settlement, and pledged bilaterally as collateral. These aren’t theoretical use cases—they demonstrate the real-world potential of tokenization. Unfortunately, regulatory approval for products with this kind of functionality remains limited, which constrains innovation.

Ultimately, our goal is to modernize finance for all investors, not just those who meet certain income thresholds. We remain hopeful that, through continued education and engagement with regulators, a pathway will emerge that balances wider access with appropriate investor protections.

Q9: How would you compare the U.S. regulatory environment with other jurisdictions where Superstate operates?

A9: We focus primarily on the U.S. because we believe it will be the heart of digital asset markets in the long run. That said, we are encouraged by the progress being made in jurisdictions like Singapore and Hong Kong, which are actively developing frameworks that support innovation.

Closing Wisdom

Q10: In closing, what’s one piece of advice you would like to share with readers who are building or thinking of building their own crypto project?

A10: Get yourself a great legal counsel — both in-house and external. Having the right guidance early on can save you from costly surprises later. The regulatory landscape is tricky, and a good lawyer will help you stay focused on building while steering clear of potential pitfalls.
