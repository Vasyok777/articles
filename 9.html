Analyst Discussion: Ethena

Kinji Steimetz
Kunal Goel
Dustin Teander

Feb 23, 2024   9 min read

Prompt

Is a decentralized stablecoin necessary for the crypto space, and could Ethena be the solution?

Primer

Stablecoin Overview

Stablecoins have emerged as one of the most successful products in crypto, serving to digitize and export USD exposure onchain with a total market capitalization of approximately $140 billion. The stablecoin market is broadly categorized into centralized and decentralized variants, each presenting distinct advantages and drawbacks.

Centralized stablecoins, notably exemplified by USDT and USDC, have rapidly secured substantial market share owing to their scalability and stability. These coins maintain their peg through a collateral basket redeemable for stablecoins, allowing for easy peg maintenance through arbitrageurs. However, the centralized model introduces notable risks.

Transparency is lower as collateral is held offchain. Despite periodic audits, centralized stablecoins merely replicate traditional financial system transparency rather than enhancing it.

Another risk is censorship resistance, with a single entity issuing these coins capable of unilaterally blacklisting addresses from interacting with the stablecoin. Circle, for instance, has already banned 212 addresses from using USDC, primarily to limit bad actors. But, this also creates a potential regulatory pressure point. Regulators could target centralization points, such as traditional banking rails, potentially impeding stablecoin providers' access to markets crucial for growing their collateral base.

Further, offchain collateral can be seized, lost, or otherwise impaired. With the failure of Silicon Valley Bank in March 2023, USDC had depegged as some of its reserves were held with the failing bank.

Decentralized stablecoins, on the other hand, aim to address the limitations of centralized stablecoins by operating entirely onchain, removing the necessity for a centralized entity. However, decentralized stablecoin come with their own set of challenges, including scalability issues, mechanism design flaws, and a lack of embedded yield. Overcollateralized stablecoins like Makers DAI tied to crypto's onchain leverage demand struggled to scale, resorting to onboarding U.S. Treasuries at the expense of transparency. Algorithmic stablecoins, such as Terra, faced inherent fragility and instability in their mechanism designs, casting doubt on their sustainability and scalability. Previous attempts with delta-neutral synthetic dollars also faced scaling difficulties due to reliance on decentralized trading venues with insufficient liquidity.

Source: Ethena

Enter Ethena

The ideal stablecoin is one that combines the strengths of both centralized and decentralized variants, offering transparency, censorship resistance, scalability, and stability. Ethena, launched on February 19, positions itself as a potential solution with unique mechanics.

Users deposit stETH into the Ethena protocol to mint USDe at a 1:1 USD ratio. The deposited stETH is sent to a third-party custodian, maintaining collateral on centralized exchanges through "Off-exchange Settlement." Ethena subsequently opens a short-ETH perpetual position on a CEX, ensuring the collateral value remains delta neutral or constant in USD terms. The funding rate interest revenue from the perpetual position is then paid out to USDe stakers effectively tokenizing the ETH carry trade.

Source: Ethena

So far, this design has attracted over $400 million in TVL with a marketed +27% APY. However, for some, the high APY has triggered flashbacks to Terras manufactured 20% APY leading to knee-jerk criticisms.

Critics express reservations about the degree of decentralization in Ethena, highlighting lingering custody risks even though the custody is with more trusted crypto custodians. Additionally, there is concern that exchanges might have the ability to block Ethena from accessing hedging markets. Skeptics also challenge the assumptions regarding positive yield, as Ethena relies on a consistent net yield from stETH and funding rates. The potential for prolonged negative funding rates poses a risk of a gradual unwinding of the stablecoin, with users opting to sell the coin rather than enduring negative or zero yields. The protocol contends that there has not been a prolonged period of negative net yield. Ethena incorporates an insurance fund to cover losses resulting from negative yield until market conditions improve or the fund is depleted.

Source: Ethena

Analyst Opinion

Kinji

There are two perspectives to consider when evaluating Ethena: one as a new stablecoin and the other as a structured product. As a stablecoin, Ethena seems to offer limited innovation, operating as a semi-decentralized alternative for stablecoin users. It aims to strike a balance between the centralization of popular stablecoins and the decentralization of alternatives, occupying a unique position on the centralization-scalability spectrum. However, the demand for such a semi-decentralized stablecoin is uncertain, especially when compared to existing options like DAI, which already serves a similar niche. While the design of USDe is not inherently flawed, it may struggle to surpass existing alternatives and cannot scale to the size of centralized competitors.

A more intriguing perspective is to view Ethena as a structured product, allowing users to maintain a delta-neutral yield position with on-chain composability. Initially supporting native ETH yield, its potential expansion into markets with attractive yield and a liquid hedging market enhances its versatility. The crucial question for Ethena revolves around the demand for this strategy, both as a passive yield product and a composable carry trade.

Another concern with Ethena is its value proposition once the carry trade ceases to be an enticing opportunity. Currently, the carry trade is perceived as a 'free lunch' in a market that prioritizes price speculation over capturing a 20-30% return on USD. Ethena has designed a mechanism to passively capture this value. However, as Ethena succeeds, it will contribute to more efficient markets, reducing demand for the carry trade and potentially impacting the attractiveness of its core product. This results in Ethena having a medium-term opportunity to bootstrap the protocol using the carry trade. However, it remains unclear how the protocol will grow once that trade reaches equilibrium. Long-term success is likely dependent on identifying and integrating new opportunities, as well as securing partnerships to dissuade users from executing these trades independently. The protocol may experience rapid adoption as depositors capitalize on an inefficient market to secure a high yield on USD, but it may struggle to find new attractive opportunities to sustain growth once markets approach efficiency.  Key questions investors should consider is the duration of the attractiveness of the carry trade and whether they believe Ethena can consistently discover new opportunities once the appeal of the carry trade diminishes.

Kunal

Ethena is the New Yearn

I agree with Kinjis framing that Ethena must be thought of as two things - a stablecoin and then a structured derivatives product.

First, lets evaluate Ethena as a stablecoin. A stablecoin must have certain properties to be useful - a medium of exchange, a unit of account, a store of value, and censorship-resistant with transparent onchain collateral. Decentralized stablecoins fail to impress here.

  • The most common medium of exchange and unit of account in crypto are either centralized stablecoins like USDT and USDC or the native asset for the chain. In 2020, there were deep pools on Uniswap against DAI, but these have moved towards centralized stablecoins. Additionally, Ethereum is one of the only major chains with a relatively high decentralized stablecoin share of ~10%. Alt-L1s have an even lower share of decentralized stables, like Solana with 1%.
  • Both Makers DAI and Ethenas USDe do not have fully transparent onchain collateral. They require some trusted custody, making them similar to centralized stablecoins in this regard.
  • Unlike their centralized counterparts, decentralized stablecoins have not blacklisted any addresses. At this stage of crypto, when there are hacks aplenty, this is not strictly superior, in my opinion.
  • Store of value is a good value proposition for decentralized stablecoins. Makers DAI pays out a saving rate of 5% through Spark, while there is no native yield for centralized stablecoins. Ethenas USDe is currently paying ~27% by capturing ETHs staking yield and shorter yield. This is valuable if it is sustainable i.e. the arbitrage continues to exist as USDe grows in supply.

As a structured product, Ethenas USDe looks more attractive. A good framing is that it is a new-age Yearn Finance vault that captures the inefficiency between ETH staking and funding rates to generate yield. In fact, like Yearn vaults, Ethena or a fork should aim to capture the many more inefficiencies that exist. Over the long run and with enough size, perhaps these opportunities will shrink (as they did for Yearn), but until then, it can provide delta-neutral yield.

Ethenas breakthrough innovation is the ability to interact with centralized exchanges, which allows it access to a much larger market. This access was not available to similar protocols like UXD earlier. It has also launched at a much better time in the market, at what seems to be the beginning of a bull market. Funding rates may remain positive as traders increase their long exposure. However, exposure to centralized exchanges brings counterparty risks atop the smart contract risk already present in most DeFi products.

Dustin

Ethena is effectively a structured carry trade product tokenized into a stablecoin so that it is maximally composable with the rest of DeFi as noted by Kinji and Kunal. There are returns within this product evidenced by the current 27% yield meaning there will likely be demand for the stablecoin (creating a reason to hold the stablecoin is the ultimate objective of a stablecoin protocol).

However, the question is how much can the product scale?

Other stablecoin protocols like Maker are generally limited in scale due to the collateral requirements which need an amount more than a dollar collateralized to mint a dollar a tough proposition. Ethena is unique in that the main constraint for its scalability (increasing the stablecoin supply) will be the external open interest in ETH perpetual markets. This is why partnering with CEXs to get exposure there is so important. But is it enough to scale to be the size of DAI ($5B) much less USDC ($26B) or USDT ($97B)?

Currently, not really.

Source: Coinglass

There is roughly $10 billion in ETH open interest (OI) across CEXs and the all-time-high ETH OI is $13 billion. Ethena scaling into DAIs size with the current market structure would mean adding $5 billion of short-side interest on top of only $10 billion outstanding long and short OI. This would result in a decrease in funding rates (if not pushing heavy negative) which dampens the yields for Ethena.

Once Ethena scales to the point of pushing funding rates to near neutral, it loses steam scalability-wise since the attractive rate drawing in deposits collapses.

So while creating a tokenized, composable ETH carry trade token is valuable, the expectation shouldnt be that it will immediately scale into being one of the largest stables. Without unprecedented growth in ETH OI (this is definitely possible especially with an ETF), the prospects should remain relatively muted (in the $1 billion ballpark feels about the ceiling in the current market).
