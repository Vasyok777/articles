Fundraising: Q1 2024

Matt Kreiser

2 days ago ⋅  17 min read

Key Insights

  • Fundraising volume decreased 23% QoQ, largely due to an outlier month of fundraising last November in Q4, which saw large raises by large bitcoin mining operations.
  • Early-stage volume and deal count increased 73% and 53% QoQ, respectively, signaling a strong appetite for new dealsin the bull market.
  • The largest deals this quarter by amount raised include Eigen Labs ($100 million), Hashkey Group ($100 million), Optimism ($89 million), Freechat ($80 million), and Zama ($73 million).

The number of deals grew by 45% in Q4, though the amount raised dropped by 23% QoQ, falling to just under $3 billion. Finance, networks, and infrastructure dominated deal flow, collectively accounting for more than 61% of quarterly fundraising volume. Additionally, the top three deals were each from one of these categories: finance (HashKey Group), infrastructure (Eigen Labs), and networks (Optimism). Top Q1 deals previously covered in our January and February fundraising reports include:

  • Eigen Labs: $100 million Series B round exclusively funded by a16z crypto. Eigen Labs is behind the development of EigenLayer and EignenDA.
  • HashKey Group: $100 million Series A round (lead undisclosed). HashKey Group is a digital asset financial services provider operating out of Hong Kong.
  • Freechat: $80 million Series A round (lead undisclosed). Freechat offers an end-to-end encrypted social chat feature via applications currently in alpha and beta.

Optimism

Optimism, an Ethereum Layer- 2 scaling solution, raised $89 million in a private token sale with undisclosed participants. While the use of funds has not been disclosed, tokens are subject to a two-year lockup, though purchasers can delegate tokens to “unaffiliated third parties for participation in governance” during the lockup period.

The bull case: Following Ethereum’s Dencun upgrade on March 13, 2024, transaction fees on Op Chains have fallen significantly from close to $1 down to roughly five cents (or less), potentially attracting more retail users. Moreover, Base, Coinbase’s Layer-2, built using the OP Stack, distributes 2.5% of revenue or 15% of net profits (whichever is greater) to the Optimism Collective. With Base activity at record highs and Coinbase set to introduce a smart wallet feature that will allow users to operate onchain from the Coinbase application using account funds, Base (and all Op Chains) could see even higher levels of usage, directly benefiting Optimism.

The bear case: Layer-2 UX remains cumbersome, with bridging and fragmented liquidity a pain point for both users and developers.

While marginal improvements have been made in regard to bridging and composability, fragmentation remains inherent to Ethereum’s architecture. As a result, networks that can handle all aspects of operation at the base layer, like Solana, may continue to see increased adoption at Ethereum, and thus Optimism’s expense. Moreover, Optimism faces stiff competition from other Ethereum Layer-2s, such as Arbitrum and Starknet.

Zama

Zama raised $73 million in a Series A round led by Multicoin and Protocol Labs. Zama is a cryptography company developing Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) for blockchain and AI. Unlike most current data management, where data is encrypted in transit but decrypted in processing, FHE enables data to remain encrypted in transit and during processing.

The bull case: Zama offers a suite of open-source FHE libraries for developers that can be implemented without cryptography expertise. By making FHE generally accessible, Zama may pave the way for FHE to finally be adopted for long-touted use cases such as confidential data sharing between healthcare providers or biometric authentication without revealing a user’s identity. Regardless, Zama has already seen adoption of fhEVM, its confidential smart contract solution. For example, Inco, highlighted in our February report, is building a confidential execution layer powered by fhEVM to perform computations over encrypted data. Other touted onchain use cases include confidential tokens, onchain identities, gameplay attributes, and RWA tokenization. By launching first, Zama could claim a first-mover advantage were FHE sees increasing adoption.

The bear case: While Zama offers FHE solutions for confidential machine learning and confidential smart contracts, it remains to be seen whether prospective customers will find them desirable (i.e., is there really product-market fit?). Since the early 2000s, a slew of legacy tech companies, from IBM to SAP, have retained patents on HME, yet adoption has yet to follow. Additionally, HME has historically had a number of technical limitations:

  • Limited Computation: can only support simple mathematical operations and comparisons.
  • Performance Overhead: leads to high costs, low scalability operations with low transactions per second (TPS) — cannot be used to train ML models on encrypted data sets.
  • Key Management: managing encryption keys can be challenging, especially with multiple parties.
  • Incompatible with Most Protocols: cannot integrate with existing protocols, given most cannot compute on encrypted data.

These limitations may hamper mass adoption for end-users, as opposed to mere technical adoption by developers like Inco, highlighted above.

Deal Trends by Investment Stage

Although total fundraising volume fell in Q1, early-stage volume, and deal count grew markedly by 73% and 53%, respectively. Specifically, both pre-seed and Series A deal volume in March was its largest (by almost double) of any other month in the last year. In Q1, 244 pre-seed deals through Series A accounted for $1.45 billion in fundraising. Notable early-stage rounds were concentrated among the finance, networks, and infrastructure sectors, including three rounds previously covered in our January and February fundraising reports:

  • Backpack: $17 million Series A round exclusively led by Placeholder VC. Backpack’s core products include the Backpack Exchange and Backpack non-custodial wallet. It is also the developer of the MadLads NFT collection on Solana, the Solana development framework Anchor, and the executable NFT (xNFT) standard on Solana, which enables developers to launch executable code as NFTs.
  • Ethena: $14 million Seed round led by Dragonfly Capital. Ethena is a capital-efficient synthetic dollar protocol on Ethereum that aims to maintain a relatively stable peg to the U.S. Dollar with its synthetic dollar, USDe.
  • Etherfi: $27 million Series A round led by Bullish and Coinfund. EtherFi is an Ethereum staking and EigenLayer native restaking solution aiming to maximize non-custodial staking.

Eclipse Labs

Eclipse Labs raised $50 million in a Series A round led by Placeholder and Hack VC. Eclipse Labs is developing Eclipse, an Ethereum Layer-2 built using the Solana Virtual Machine (SVM), with Celestia as its data availability layer that is intended to be Ethereum’s “fastest” Layer-2, handling “99%” of use cases.

The bull case: In the competition between Ethereum Layer 2’s, a first-mover advantage does not seem to exist, at least in regard to token value accrual or network activity, which bodes well for Eclipse. Case in point, Mantle, whose MNT token again became the largest Layer-2 token by market cap in March. After rebranding from BitDAO in May 2023, Mantle launched its mainnet alpha in July 2023 and has since ridden the bull market and a beating drum of incentive programs backed by its treasury (most notably with Ethena Labs) to be the Layer-2 token with the highest circulating market cap. In contrast, Base, which has no accompanying token, may have the highest network activity of all Layer-2’s and remains red hot due in large part to general bullishness on Coinbase, and retail participation.

Mantle and Base are both built with the OP Stack, a technical standard users have become accustomed to, but which has also seen failed transactions during peaks of transaction activity, such as periods of memecoin mania on Base. Effectively, this drives the need for execution layer innovation, which Eclipse is aiming for. If Eclipse can deliver on its technical goal of building a Layer-2 as performant as Solana, it could offer a superior user experience in regard to speed and transaction cost, as well as handle the next order of magnitude of retail participants onchain. Couple that technical delivery with strong BD and incentive programs, and there is no reason Eclipse, scheduled for a Q2 mainnet launch, can’t be one of the top Ethereum Layer-2’s in terms of network activity or token value accrual within the next year.

The bear case: The narrative told in the above bull case is largely dependent on a bull market. Pull the bull market, and the speculative network activity and incentive programs largely disappear. That still leaves Eclipse with a potentially superior technical stack, but that superiority may not be discernible enough to attract new users in a bear market where reduced network activity makes performance at scale irrelevant, leading to an undifferentiated user experience.

0G Labs

0G Labs raised $35 million in a Pre-Seed round led by Hack VC to build a modular “AI” chain. Specifically, 0G Labs is developing ZeroGravity (aka 0G), a data availability system with a built-in general-purpose storage layer. This design minimizes data transfer volume required for broadcast and thereby optimizes for a scalable Data Availability (DA) service layer.

The bull case: Modular infrastructure layers for rollups may be the hottest fundraising ticket in private crypto markets. As mentioned in our February report, Celestia indicates the potential for an early market size, having raised $55 million in private funding, while TIA currently boasts a $2.1 billion market cap ($12.4 billion FDV). This interest is based on the benefits of building on a modular stack that separates execution from consensus and data availability (DA). Like prospective competitors Celestia, EigenLayer, Near, and Avail, 0G enables developers to focus on building products that may require more specialized infrastructure than a generalized Layer-1 while still abstracting away the complexities of DA and consensus. Investor press releases such as OKX Ventures tout “horizontal scaling” as the feature that differentiates 0G, resulting in “a throughput of 50GBs per consensus layer… [and] costs that are 100 times cheaper than existing systems.”

The bear case: While Celestia may show the potential for 0G’s future market size, it has also already captured a significant portion of the market as the first modular infrastructure layer. If that’s the case, 0G will be reliant on showing it is differentiated enough to convince existing Celestia users to switch to 0G.

Regardless, the modular stack still has to prove its utility for various crypto use cases, especially ones that require low latency. It’s not evident that cross-rollup communication is seamless enough to facilitate transactions like flash loans. DeFi accounts for most blockchain activity and functions well on a high-throughput base layer like Solana. It is yet to be determined if a modular stack could better support DeFi or other low-latency uses that require integrated liquidity.

Infrastructure Sector

Infrastructure deal volume fell 64% QoQ, following an outlier month of fundraising in November of Q4 driven by large deals for bitcoin miners. Outside of this outlier month, infrastructure fundraising volume remained steady, with deal count increasing 47% QoQ. Top Q1 deals already covered in our January and February fundraising reports include the aforementioned Eigen Labs and Etherfi deals, as well as Core Scientific, a previously bankrupt North American-based Bitcoin miner that raised $55 million in a strategic round.

IO Research

IO Research raised $30 million in a Series A round led by Hack VC. IO Research is the developer of io.net, a DePIN project deployed on Solana that aggregates GPU resources for machine learning and AI use cases. Like its competitors Render and Akash, io.net acts as a two-sided market consisting of a supply of physical compute resources and demand-side users who consume the compute resources.

The bull case: Increased demand for high-performance computing is a rising tide that is lifting all boats. Even if io.net is unable to source at scale A100s and H100s, which are best in class for machine learning workloads, consumer-grade GPUs, like the RTX 3090, of which io.net has amassed more than 34,000, remain viable for machine learning, although less performant. io.net can offer these GPUs at a scale its competitors can’t. As of the date of this report, io.net has more than 398,000 GPUs, while its next closest competitor, Render Network, has just over 10,000.

The bear case: Decentralized GPU networks face constraints on both the supply and demand side. In regard to the supply side, io.net markets itself as a GPU market for machine learning, but as of the date of this report possesses just 514 H100 GPUs. As a point of comparison, Factorial Funds estimated OpenAI uses between 4,200 to 10,500 H100s to run Sora, its text-to-video model. In regard to the demand side, while decentralized compute networks typically market themselves as cheaper alternatives to centralized competitors, Messari has previously reported on how cost is not the primary concern for customers, for which service level agreements, quality of service, and competitive advantages are often prioritized. This leaves decentralized providers like io.net uncompetitive in market conditions where customers are willing and eager to pay more. For AI and machine learning, such market conditions could persist well into the next decade.

Succinct

Succinct raised $55 million in a Series A round led by Paradigm. Succinct is focused on making zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs) accessible to developers. At the moment, it does so via Succint Processor 1 (SP1), a zero-knowledge virtual machine (zkVM) first introduced in February that verifies the execution of arbitrary Rust programs. Succinct is also developing Succinct Network, an application-specific chain operating as a prover network.

The bull case: Succinct Network is an under-development prover network that can provide proof generation for open-source proof systems. In a future where ZKP is widely adopted, but technically challenging to implement, Succinct is positioned to capture market share.

The bear case: Over time, ZKPs may become default accessible as developers become increasingly proficient in the technology. In such a world, prover networks become redundant as provers are commoditized along with the rest of the ZKP.

Financial Sector

While financial deal volume fell 17% QoQ, the sector was still the most funded in crypto, with $708 million in deal volume in Q1. Deal counts also increased by 67% QoQ. Notable Q1 financial deals covered in our January and February fundraising reports include the aforementioned Backpack deal as well as:

  • dYdX Foundation: $30 million in grant funding from the dYdX Community Treasury. dYdX Chain is an application-specific blockchain that supports its decentralized derivatives protocol.
  • Flowdesk:  $50 million Series B round led by Cathay Innovation. Flowdesk is a crypto market maker and OTC trading firm.
  • MetaStreet: $25 million raised (round and lead investor(s) undisclosed). MetaStreet is a lending and liquid token protocol that organizes deposited capital into various pools based on risk profiles and assigns lenders ERC-20 “liquid credit tokens” (LCTs) representing their position within the pool. Borrowers post NFTs as collateral.

Figure Markets

Figure Markets raised $60 million in a Series A round led by Jump Crypto, Pantera Capital, and Lightspeed. Figure Markets is a spinout of Figure, a financial services company that is primarily a home equity line of credit lender. Today, Figure Markets allows U.S. residents to borrow against crypto holdings and intends to also offer trading and investment services for individuals and institutions.

The bull case: Figure’s existing consumer customer base and strong brand recognition may prove useful in attracting and generating a large crypto lending book in the U.S. On top of this, planned offerings such as using Figure Market’s REIT to collateralize a BTC trade could attract more sophisticated users, expanding Figure Market’s customer base. Backing from large crypto-native firms is also a positive signal as Figure Markets attempts to attract a new customer base to products and services typically reserved for more sophisticated crypto-native users onchain. Further, plans to make FTX creditors owners in Figure Markets nicely round out a go-to-market strategy heavily reliant on branding and marketing.

The bear case: While Figure Markets may see an opportunity in the U.S. market following the collapse of CeFi players such as Celsius, BlockFi, and Voyager last cycle, extensive competition remains. Coinbase Prime began offering institutional lending in August, while NEXO continues to offer crypto lending, borrowing, and trading. Beyond this, the secular trend of increasing retail flows onchain inevitably leads to increased deposits to permissionless non-custodial money markets like Aave, which offer lower fees and 24/7 accessibility by default. Last cycle, CeFi players might have been able to rely on poor onchain UX/UI to keep offchain non-native crypto users segmented from native-crypto users, but with each iterative improvement onchain, that market opportunity is being abstracted away.

Networks Sector

Networks deal volume increased 65% QoQ, with March of Q1 the largest networks sector fundraising month in the last year. Deal count also increased 100% QoQ, with all notable rounds taking place in March. The aforementioned Eclipse, Optimism, and 0G Labs deals were accompanied by other notable networks deals encompassing Layer 1’s.

Berachain

Berachain raised $69 million in an undisclosed round led by Brevan Howard Digital and Framework Ventures. Berachain is an EVM-compatible Layer 1 chain built using the Cosmos SDK that uses a novel Proof-of-Liquidity (PoL) mechanism, whereby validators reward liquidity providers with the network’s BGT token. Mainnet launch is expected in Q2 2024.

The bull case: Proof-of-Liquidity (PoL) is a novel iteration of Proof-of-Stake (PoS) that separates functions of a traditional PoS native token into two tokens, a delegation token (BGT) and a gas token (BERA).

On Berachain, users earn the Bera Governance Token (BGT) by providing liquidity to the Berachain Exchange (BEX). Users can then delegate BGT to validators, with validators (i) producing blocks based on the proportional weight of BGT delegated to them and (ii) voting on BGT inflation across any number of liquidity pools. Bribes are also distributed by validators to their delegators (if the validator has created them). If successful, Berachain’s PoL may address issues such as stake centralization while inherently increasing and sustaining network liquidity, creating a flywheel for continued network growth.

The bear case: There is no guarantee that Berachain’s PoL creates a flywheel for economic network growth or sustains network liquidity. PoL is untested at scale and may have unforeseen vulnerabilities or exploitations upon mainnet launch. Moreover, while PoL may mitigate some aspects of stake centralization, it increases the consequences of any exploits of Berachain Exchange (BEX), the network’s native DEX.

peaq

Peaq raised $15 million in an undisclosed round led by Generative Ventures and Borderless Capital. Peaq is a Layer 1 substrate chain designed for Decentralized Physical Infrastructure Networks (DePINs), with backend features including self-sovereign Machine IDs and role-based access control. It plans to use the funding to incentivize ecosystem growth and build out modular DePIN functions as peaq prepares its token offering and network launch.

The bull case: Peaq aims to become the go-to hub for DePINs within Polkadot's ecosystem, offering solutions including:

  • Machine-focused functions, including self-sovereign identities for machines/devices, role-based access control, geolocation services, and a machine-to-X payment system
  • A secure and tamper-proof way to verify device data
  • A control center to easily onboard and connect a device to any DePIN network on peaq

Peaq foresees a world in which the Web2-based Internet of Things (IoT) is transformed into the Web3-based Economy of Things and seeks to be the Layer-1 network facilitating it. With over 20 DePins joining peaq in the last year in areas including ridesharing, health data, and peer-to-peer electric vehicle charging, the project team is making sustained progress toward this goal.

The bear case: Today’s more widely adopted DePIN projects typically opt to exist on popular Layer-1 chains like, for example, Render and Helium on Solana or their own chains like Akash or Bittensor. For peaq to realize its goal as the go-to-hub for DePINs, it will need to convince top projects why they should make peaq home. While projects like Render have shifted from Ethereum to Solana for performance reasons, it remains to be seen if top DePIN projects will ever opt for the middle ground of building on top of a Layer-1 network like Peaq, rather than opting for a widely adopted network like Solana or simply building their own chain like Akash.

Concluding Thoughts

Q1 saw markedly increased investment volume in networks with continued strength in the financial and infrastructure sectors. Moreover, deal counts rose by at least 30% across all sectors, signaling increased investor appetite during the bull market. Overall, private crypto fundraising markets continue to show sustained strength heading into the BTC halving, potential ETH ETFs, and forecasted rate cuts in June.
