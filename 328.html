The Week in Crypto Policy: House Committee Chairs & Commissioner Crenshaw Vote

George Leonardo

Dec 13, 2024 ⋅  11 min read

Key Takeaways

  • Representatives with pro-crypto track records are expected to chair key committees of jurisdiction next Congress after a week of House Steering Committee elections.
  • SEC Commissioner Caroline Crenshaw’s confirmation is in limbo after Senate Banking Chairman Sherrod Brown (D-OH) was forced to delay the committee vote, first citing “attendance issues,” and then blaming “corporate special interests” for the delay.
  • Senator Tim Scott (R-SC) discussed debanking at a Senate Banking hearing, and a federal district court ruled the FDIC needs to be more transparent with letters the agency sent to banks in 2022 ordering them to pause all crypto-related activity.
  • Rep. Mike Carey (R-OH) is asking Treasury and the IRS to refrain from finalizing any rule that would apply digital asset broker tax reporting requirements to self-custody wallet providers.

House Committee Chair Break Downs

This week, the House Steering Committee voted to select committee chairs for the 119th Congress. The nominees are expected to be officially ratified by the House Republican Conference next week.

Here’s a look at who will be chairing House Financial Services, House Agriculture, and House Energy & Commerce.

House Financial Services

Incoming Chair: Rep. French Hill (R-AR)

Jurisdiction: “HFSC” jurisdiction covers banks, the economy, securities, public and private housing, and urban development. HFSC also has oversight authority over banking regulators (Federal Reserve, FDIC, OCC), Treasury, and the Securities and Exchange Commission.

Rep. Hill On Crypto:

Rep. Hill has been one of the most active members on crypto policy the last few years. As Chair of HFSC’s subcommittee on digital assets, Rep. Hill held hearings regularly covering crypto-related issues, from stablecoins and market structure, to combatting illicit finance and promoting innovation generally.

Like Chair McHenry, Rep. Hill will likely keep digital assets as an HFSC priority and work to build bipartisan support for meaningful crypto legislation that can get signed into law.

Rep. Hill has also made clear he will prioritize halting, reversing, and investigating Operation Choke Point 2.0.

Legislation Sponsored or Cosponsored (118th Congress) by Rep. Hill:

  • ​FIT 21 (Comprehensive market structure bill)
  • ​Keep Innovation in America Act:
    • A bill to clarify and narrow the scope of digital asset broker reporting requirements, and direct Treasury to study the impact of another IIJA provision that requires reporting of certain digital asset transactions of greater than $10,000.
  • A SAB 121 repeal bill and SAB 121 repeal resolution.
  • ​CBDC Anti-Surveillance State Act:
    • A bill to prohibit the Fed & Treasury from issuing a CBDC.
  • ​Power of the Mint Act:
    • A bill to prohibit the Fed and Treasury from issuing a CBDC without Congressional approval.
  • ​21st Century Dollar Act:
    • A bill to require Treasury and the Federal Reserve to implement a strategy to ensure the dollar remains the primary global reserve currency.
    • This would include reports on foreign central banks' efforts to create a CBDC and the use of the renminbi in international payments.
  • ​Chinese CBDC Prohibition Act of 2023:
    • A bill to prohibit money services businesses from engaging in transactions involving a Chinese CBDC.

Votes:

In committee, Rep. Hill voted to advance the Keep Your Coins Act (a pro-self-custody bill), FIT 21 (market structure), stablecoin legislation, a SAB 121 repeal, the CBCD Anti-Surveillance State Act, and the Financial Technology Protection Act.

On the House floor, Rep. Hill voted for FIT 21, SAB 121 repeal, and the CBDC Anti-Surveillance State Act (the three major bills that made it to the floor).

Other Priorities: In addition to digital assets and addressing OCP 2.0, Rep. Hill has outlined a detailed set of principles to “Make Community Banking Great Again.” According to the plan, Rep. Hill wants to tailor banking regulations to better account for the size and activities of the specific bank, make it easier for banks to merge, acquire, or partner with other banks or nonbanks, and expand smaller banks’ access to funding and capital.

House Agriculture Committee

Returning Chair: Rep. Glenn “GT” Thompson (R-PA).

  • Note: Chair Thompson’s bid to serve another term as House Ag Chair was unopposed, so there was no Steering Committee election for this race.

Jurisdiction: House “Ag” jurisdiction spans commodities, forestry, nutrition, livestock, rural development, and of course, agriculture. House Ag oversees the Commodities Futures Trading Commission, which has jurisdiction over derivatives markets, including commodity futures, options, and swaps. It also has broad enforcement authority to police against fraud and manipulation in commodity markets.

Rep. Thompson On Crypto:

Rep. Thompson has been a leader on crafting legislation to regulate the digital commodity spot market.

For example, going back to the 117th Congress, Chair Thompson introduced the Digital Commodity Exchange Act (“DCEA”), which would have granted the CFTC jurisdiction over the digital commodity spot market and required exchanges to comply with core principles aimed at ensuring market integrity.

Similarly, in the 118th Congress, Rep. Thompson played a leading role in crafting FIT 21’s provisions governing digital commodities and related intermediaries (drawing from the DCEA).

Votes:

Under Rep. Thompson’s leadership, House Ag passed FIT 21 out of committee by a bipartisan voice vote.

On the House floor, Rep. Thompson voted in favor of FIT 21, SAB 121 repeal, and the CBDC Anti-Surveillance State Act.

Other Priorities:

The Farm Bill. Congress is expected to pass a one-year extension of the farm bill by year-end, which means Ag committees will once again be busy working on a longer-term authorization next year. The Farm Bill is pretty much always a priority for committee members. For example, see House Ag member Rep. Brad Finstad (R-MN) chiding Senate Ag for working on crypto before finalizing a farm bill reauthorization earlier this year.

House Energy & Commerce

Incoming Chair: Rep. Brett Guthrie (R-KY).

Jurisdiction: House “E&C” contains the broadest jurisdiction of committees, covering health care, energy, telecom, the internet, privacy, and interstate and foreign commerce. E&C oversees the Federal Trade Commission, HHS, the FDA, and the Department of Commerce.

This Congress, E&C held hearings and advanced legislation addressing uses of blockchain technology in commerce.

Rep. Guthrie on Crypto:

While crypto hasn’t been a focus of Rep. Guthrie’s tenure, he is an original cosponsor of the bipartisan Consumer Safety Technology Act.

This bill would:

  • Establish a pilot program to explore the use of AI to support the Consumer Product Safety Commission,
  • Direct the Department of Commerce to study and report on how blockchain can be used to protect consumers, and
  • Direct the Federal Trade Commission to report on efforts to prevent fraud involving digital tokens.
  • ​Text.

Votes:

The House passed the Consumer Safety Technology Act by voice vote earlier this year, but it remains stalled in the Senate.

As for bills outside of E&C’s jurisdiction, Rep. Guthrie voted for FIT 21, SAB 121 repeal, and the CBDC Anti-Surveillance State Act (also a cosponsor) on the House floor.

Other Priorities:

President-elect Trump and House leadership have made clear they will prioritize increasing U.S. energy supply as part of reconciliation efforts next year.

As for tech issues, Punchbowl reports Rep. Guthrie’s priorities may include: “Beating China to 6G, connecting more Americans to broadband, and addressing Big Tech’s treatment of conservative speech.”

Senate Banking Delays Vote on Commissioner Crenshaw

Early Wednesday morning, Senate Banking was scheduled to vote on SEC Commissioner Caroline Crenshaw’s nomination to serve another term as Commissioner.

At the start of the hearing, however, Senate Banking Chairman Sherrod Brown (D-OH) announced the vote was moved to the afternoon, citing attendance issues.

But Brown was unable to secure a unanimous consent (“UC”) agreement to waive a Senate procedural obstacle preventing the committee from holding the vote in the afternoon. Per Fox Business News’s Eleanor Terrett, Ranking Member Tim Scott (R-SC) objected to the UC request.

Shortly thereafter, Chairman Brown released a statement blaming “corporate special interests” for the delay:

  • “Republican Senators are doing the bidding of corporate special interests and standing in the way of her confirmation.”
  • ​Full Statement.

What's Next?

To lock in Commissioner Crenshaw’s confirmation, Senator Brown needs to once again reschedule the committee vote and work with Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer to find time for a final confirmation vote on the Senate floor.

With only one week left of session expected, it remains to be seen whether the renomination vote fits in the schedule—and whether Senate Democrats want to vote on a nominee drawing intense opposition from the crypto community.

Why Crypto Cares

While SEC Chair Gensler took much of the spotlight (and heat) from the crypto community over the last few years, Commissioner Crenshaw shared Chair Gensler’s skepticism of crypto and generally voted in lockstep with the outgoing Chair (see, e.g., statement in support of approving the SEC’s Dealer Rule (which was recently struck down by a district court in a challenge brought by the Blockchain Association)).

Most notably, Commissioner Crenshaw voted against approving the Grayscale spot Bitcoin ETP—even after the D.C. Circuit ruled the SEC’s initial denial was arbitrary and capricious. (Recall, even Chair Gensler voted to approve the spot ETP after the D.C. court’s ruling).

In her dissent, Commissioner Crenshaw primarily cited concerns with fraud and manipulation in the Bitcoin spot market, while also raising illicit finance concerns and FTX’s collapse in the context of protecting the public interest.

In light of Commissioner Crenshaw’s track record on crypto, industry groups and Stand With Crypto are opposing her nomination.

For example, here are letters from the Blockchain Association & DeFi Education Fund and The Digital Chamber urging Senators to oppose Commissioner Crenshaw's nomination.

​Stand With Crypto has also created a tool to make it easier for individual crypto advocates to let their Senators (and their Legislative Correspondents) know they oppose Commissioner Crenshaw’s nomination.

Look Ahead

Congress

  • The House passed the NDAA; Senate to take it up early next week.
  • House and Senate need to pass a government funding bill by December 20.
  • Senate Ds may try to confirm more nominations.

IRS

  • Still keeping eyes out for an IRS final rule regarding noncustodial digital asset brokers.
    • (See Rep. Mike Carey's letter below urging IRS to refrain from finalizing these rules).
  • December 19 - 2pm ET: IRS to host webinar for tax practitioners on:
    • “Digital assets existing and new reporting requirements.”

Quick Hits

Operation Choke Point 2.0

Senate Banking Hearing

  • In opening remarks at a Senate Banking hearing with the CFPB on consumer protection issues, Ranking Member Tim Scott (R-SC) voiced concerns with recent reports of debanking, highlighting his previous efforts “to call out banking agencies for weaponizing their power and private institutions for bending to powerful interests in Washington.”
  • Senator Scott added:
    • “No legal business should ever be debanked.”

FOIA - FDIC Crypto Pause Letters

  • As a result of FOIA litigation supported by Coinbase, the FDIC publicly released letters that were sent to certain banks in 2022.
  • Though heavily redacted, the letters show the FDIC told certain banks to pause all crypto-asset related activity.
    • “However, at this time the FDIC has not yet determined what regulatory filings will be necessary for a bank to engage in this type of activity. As a result, we ask that you pause all crypto asset-related activity.”
    • ​Letters.
    • ​Article with more background (Turner Wright, Cointelegraph).
  • On Thursday, Judge Reyes ordered the FDIC to “re-review the documents” and “make more thoughtful redactions.”

Also on Thursday, Capitol Account's Ryan Tracy asked FDIC Chair Gruenberg about the letters. Read Gruenberg's response here.

More on Debanking

  • “Debanking hurts everyone. It's time to end it once and for all” - Op-ed by Nic Carter and Austin Campbell in Fortune Crypto (calling on President-elect Trump and the new Congress to prioritize investigating and ending debanking).
  • “Debanking (and Debunking)” - Article by Patrick McKenzie (offering alternative theories on why crypto has a banking problem and a thorough explanation of the history and process behind debanking generally).

Tax

Digital Asset Broker Tax Reporting Requirements

  • Rep. Mike Carey (R-OH) penned a letter to Treasury and the IRS urging the agencies to refrain from any further expansion of the IRS Broker Rule.
    • More specifically, the letter argues the IRS went far enough when it finalized broker reporting rules for centralized intermediaries earlier this year.
    • Thus, any efforts to finalize rules for self-custodial digital asset wallet providers and/or developers of noncustodial software would:
      • (1) Go beyond the will of Congress as codified in the IIJA, and
      • (2) Impose an impossible burden on the IRS without any corresponding benefit.
  • ​Letter.
  • Rep. Carey sits on the House Ways & Means Committee, which has jurisdiction over tax issues.

Crypto in 401(k) Plans

  • On December 4th, GAO publicly released a new report examining crypto assets in 401(k) plans.
  • In the report, GAO recommends improving disclosures around 401(k) plans’ investments in crypto assets, citing high volatility of crypto asset prices and regulatory uncertainty.
    • Report Text:
      • 401(k) Plans: Industry Data Show Low Participant Use of Crypto Assets Although DOL's Data Limitations Persist.

Opinion/SEC

  • J.W. Verret, JD/CPA & Associate Professor of Law, shares a “Top Ten List to Reform the SEC Corporation Finance Division.”
