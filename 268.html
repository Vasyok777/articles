The Week in Crypto Policy: Post-Election Analysis

George Leonardo

Nov 8, 2024 ⋅  11 min read

Key Insights

  • Compared to the Biden Administration, the Trump Administration will likely mark a significant improvement from the regulatory status quo, with the expected appointment of less crypto-hostile (if not, pro-crypto) agency personnel and increased engagement with industry stakeholders on rulemakings.
  • Republican control of the House and Senate offer several reasons to be optimistic that pro-crypto legislation reaches President Trump's desk in the 119th Congress.
  • The successes of pro-crypto candidates on both sides of the aisle, particularly in key swing states and districts, show there is likely much more to gain than lose politically by embracing crypto.

Election Analysis: Impact of White House and Congressional Races

White House

The incoming Trump Administration will likely be a significant improvement for crypto policy compared to the last few years. Under the Biden Administration, federal banking regulators have pressured banks to avoid crypto-related activities and crypto businesses, Treasury has proposed illicit finance and tax rules often decried as unworkable for decentralized technology, and the SEC has avoided issuing tailored rules for crypto in favor of bringing enforcement actions against everyone from exchanges who sought to register in good faith to NFT artists.

Based on Trump's campaign and the GOP Party's platform, the Trump Administration is likely to end this hostility and work with industry to improve the current regulatory status quo.

Here are some policy items Trump raised on the campaign trail:

  • Establish a Bitcoin and crypto advisory council dedicated to establishing clear rules of the road, composed of industry representatives
  • End the “unlawful and unAmerican Crypto crackdown”
  • Hold onto Bitcoin acquired by the federal government as a strategic reserve asset
  • Defend the right to mine Bitcoin
  • Remove SEC Chair Gensler
  • Oppose a U.S. CBDC
  • Make the U.S. the “crypto capital” of the world
  • Protect the right to self-custody and to “transact free from Government Surveillance and Control”
  • Free Ross Ulbricht, creator of the Silk Road
  • (See Bitcoin Conference speech & GOP Party Platform).

Also of note, Trump’s sons are building a DeFi project, World Liberty Financial. Expect his political opponents to raise this as a conflict-of-interest concern.

Congress

Republicans will control the Senate and most likely the House (based on current vote counts and prediction markets). This bodes well for crypto policy in the 119th Congress for a few reasons detailed below.

Senate Confirmation

For Trump to deliver on the pro-crypto promises discussed above, he'll need the help of like-minded department and agency heads (e.g., Treasury Secretary, SEC Chair, etc.). A Republican-controlled Senate means Trump should be able to get his top choices confirmed. Democrats may try to stall the process, but confirmation only requires a simple majority, and there will be at least 53 Republican Senators (plus VP J.D. Vance in the case of a tie).

Floor & Committee Agendas

Majorities in the House and Senate mean Republican leadership will control the floor agenda (i.e., when to vote on which bills), and Republican committee chairs will drive legislative and oversight functions (e.g., set markup and hearing schedules) of their respective committees.

This Congress, House Republican leadership brought a variety of crypto bills to the floor, including FIT 21, SAB 121 repeal, anti-CBDC legislation, and a slate of bills focused on commerce-based uses of blockchain technology. In contrast, the Democratic-controlled Senate has only voted on the SAB 121 repeal resolution.

At the committee level, House Financial Services Committee (“HFSC”), led by Republican Patrick McHenry, considered legislation addressing stablecoins (bipartisan), protecting self-custody (Keep Your Coins Act; only Rs supported), and more. Crypto-related hearings held by the full HFSC committee and its digital asset subcommittee often highlighted economic opportunity, while also flagging concerns with the the SEC’s regulation-by-enforcement approach. In contrast, the Democratic-controlled Senate Banking Committee failed to markup crypto-specific legislation, and committee hearings tended to focus on illicit-finance or consumer protection concerns.

Now with control of both chambers, House and Senate leadership can work more constructively on a bicameral basis on crypto legislation. For example, Senator Tim Scott (R-SC) plans to set up a digital asset subcommittee similar to HFSC’s. House Republican Majority Leader Steve Scalise has already listed crypto legislation as one of several priorities next year in a recent Dear Colleague Letter. And with evidence mounting regarding Operation Chokepoint 2.0, both HFSC and Senate Banking could conduct oversight hearings into federal banking regulators’ past and present crypto-related policies.

Budget Reconciliation & CRA

While it typically requires 60 votes to clear the Senate's filibuster, there are two ways by which Republicans could advance certain pieces of legislation with only a simple majority in the Senate: (1) budget reconciliation and (2) CRA resolutions of disapproval.

(1) Budget Reconciliation

The budget reconciliation process allows the House and Senate to pass certain budget-related items with only a simple majority.

Importantly, there are time and scope limitations on what may be included in a budget reconciliation package. For example, under Senate “Byrd rule” requirements, only provisions that impact changes in spending, revenues, and/or federal debt may be included (though Byrd rule points of order may be waived with a vote of 60 Senators).

Whether a provision violates the Byrd rule can be a contentious issue, with the Senate Parliamentarian typically being the final arbiter. Thus, it’s unclear to what extent this could be used to advance crypto-related provisions, and to what extent Republicans would want to use it as a vehicle for crypto reforms. Based on Majority Leader Steve Scalise's recent Dear Colleague Letter, the House would likely focus reconciliation efforts on Trump tax cuts, energy policy, and border security. Previously, budget reconciliation has been used to pass a variety of measures, from Affordable Care Act reforms to Trump tax cuts.

  • More background on budget reconciliation and the Byrd rule: CRS, House Budget Committee.

(2) CRA Resolutions of Disapproval

The Congressional Review Act allows Congress to effectively repeal agency rules issued within a specific time frame and prohibit federal agencies from issuing “substantially similar” rules in the future—all with a simple majority. Thus, with unified control in the House and Senate, Republicans could likely roll back any anti-crypto rules the Biden Administration attempts to finalize on the way out. Recall, the House and Senate used this process to pass the SAB 121 repeal resolution, though President Biden ultimately vetoed the measure.

Bottom Line

If a Republican Administration and Republican Senate are as active on crypto policy during the 119th Congress as the Republican House was in the 118th, it should be a busy next two years. As for the lame duck and potential compromises on major crypto legislation like market structure and stablecoins, Republicans may be more inclined to hold off until next year when they have a much stronger negotiating position.

Election Results: House & Senate Races

Here’s a rapid-fire look at some of the key wins and losses this week.

Senate

Bernie Moreno - Ohio

  • Bernie Moreno upset sitting Senate Banking Chairman Sherrod Brown, taking down one of crypto’s lead antagonists in the Senate, and adding a former blockchain-entrepreneur to the Senate’s ranks.

Tim Sheehy - Montana

  • Tim Sheehy (R), a pro-crypto advocate with an A rating from Stand with Crypto, defeated incumbent John Tester (D) in Montana. By unseating the second highest ranking member on Senate Banking, the top Democrat on Senate Banking will now be either Senator Jack Reed (unlikely given preference to stay top D on Senate Armed Services), Senator Mark Warner (unclear given potential preference to stay top D on Senate Intelligence, but also interest in financial services policy), or Senator Elizabeth Warren.

Dave McCormick – Pennsylvania

  • In the critical swing-state of Pennsylvania, pro-crypto Dave McCormick (R) defeated incumbent Senator Bob Casey (D)–retiring a cosponsor of Senator Warren’s Digital Asset Anti-Money Laundering Act.

Elizabeth Warren – Massachusetts

  • Senator Warren (D) comfortably held off a challenge from John Deaton (R): 59.6% to 40.4%. Deaton fared slightly better than last cycle’s Republican who only garnered 36% of the vote against Warren. Deaton was also outspent $25 million to $3.5 million. While Senator Warren was safe in deep blue Massachusetts, one can at least hope she reconsiders the benefits of her anti-crypto political strategy given the pro-crypto victories (and anti-crypto losses) in swing states & districts.

Elissa Slotkin – Michigan

  • Rep. Slotkin (D) will replace retiring Senator Debbie Stabenow (D). Slotkin narrowly defeated Mike Rogers (R), who also had an A rating from Stand With Crypto. Slotkin voted in favor of FIT 21 and SAB 121 repeal, but against a CBDC repeal bill.

Rick Scott – Florida

  • Rick Scott (R) handily won Florida. A long-shot candidate for Senate Majority Leader, Scott has supported various crypto bills, including the Financial Freedom Act of 2023, a bill to prohibit a U.S. CBDC, and is the solo-cosponsor of Senator Mike Lee’s Saving Privacy Act, which would significantly roll back the Bank Secrecy Act.

Jim Justice – West Virginia

  • Republican Jim Justice will replace retiring Senator Joe Manchin (D-WV). Justice has an A rating from Stand with Crypto, opposes a CBDC, and could be a leader on Bitcoin mining and related energy issues given his experience as Governor of West Virginia.

John Curtis - Utah

  • Rep. John Curtis (R) won his race to replace retiring Senator Mitt Romney in the Senate. While in the House, Curtis voted for SAB 121 repeal, and cosponsored anti-CBDC legislation and FIT 21. It's also worth flagging this race because Romney's retirement means the CANSEE Act and Terrorist Financing Prevention Act will be without one of their two Republican cosponsors next Congress.

Too Close to Call

Jacky Rosen – Nevada

  • Senator Jacky Rosen (D) is predicted to edge out challenger Captain Sam Brown (R). Both had A ratings from Stand With Crypto. Senator Rosen voted to repeal SAB 121.

Ruben Gallego – Arizona

  • Rep. Ruben Gallego (D-AZ) is currently leading Kari Lake. Rep. Gallego, who had previously been skeptical of Bitcoin and crypto, voted for FIT 21 and to repeal SAB 121 earlier this year. Both Rep. Gallego and Lake had A ratings from Stand with Crypto. The winner will replace retiring Senator Kyrsten Sinema (I-AZ), who had supported de minimis tax exemptions for crypto and a bill to prohibit deceptive advertising related to digital assets.

House

Zach Nunn - Iowa

  • Rep. Zach Nunn (R), a crypto ally backed by industry, held on in a competitive race in Iowa. Rep. Nunn has supported crypto legislation as member of House Ag and House Financial Services, including bipartisan proposals aimed at curbing illicit finance threats involving emerging technology.

New York

Mike Lawler

  • Rep. Mike Lawler (R) won a competitive New York district. Rep. Lawler is a member of HFSC and was endorsed by Stand with Crypto. He has supported stablecoin legislation, FIT 21, and SAB 121 repeal, along with cosponsoring a few illicit finance related bills.

Josh Riley (flipped Marc Molinaro (R-NY) seat).

  • AP has called the race for Josh Riley (D), who beat Rep. Molinaro (R) in a tough race for New York’s 19th Congressional district. Rep. Molinaro had supported various crypto bills, and even cosponsored FIT 21, earning an endorsement from Stand with Crypto. Riley has a B rating from Stand with Crypto – with the one knock seemingly coming from him not having bought, sold, or used blockchain assets or investment tools.

Don Davis - North Carolina

  • Rep. Don Davis (D-NC) held on in a competitive district. Davis voted for SAB 121 repeal, against FIT 21, and urged the DNC to embrace crypto policy earlier this year.

Darren Soto - Florida

  • Rep. Darren Soto (D-FL) handily won re-election. While not a surprise, it’s worth flagging because Soto sometimes gets overlooked (perhaps because he’s not on House Ag or HFSC). Soto is one of crypto's most longstanding allies in the House.
  • Specifically, Rep. Soto has cosponsored bills to protect certain blockchain developers and service providers from BSA requirements (Blockchain Regulatory Certainty Act), clarify that digital assets sold as part of an investment contract are not themselves an investment contract security (Securities Clarity Act), and direct the Department of Commerce to report on emerging uses of blockchain tech (Consumer Safety Technology Act).

House - Too Close to Call

Arizona

  • Rep. David Schweikert (R) is leading narrowly in one of the House’s more competitive districts. Schweikert is a key crypto ally on House Ways & Means, which is expected to be busy on tax policy next year. Schweikert has previously sponsored the Keep Innovation in America Act and a de minimis tax exemption for small personal crypto transactions. He also supports the Financial Freedom Act, which aims to protect individuals’ ability to invest in crypto through self-directed brokerage accounts.

Colorado

  • Rep. Yadira Caraveo (D) is narrowly ahead in her re-election bid. As the top Democrat on House Ag’s subcommittee on digital commodities, she helped shepherd bipartisan support of FIT 21. She also voted to repeal SAB 121.
