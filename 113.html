The Infrastructure vs Application Debate

Dylan Bane
Kinji Steimetz
Seth Bloomberg

10 hours ago ⋅  9 min read

Primer

Infra vs. Apps?

Once again, the crypto world is debating whether the industry should focus on investing in blockchain infrastructure or applications for end-users. This debate has persisted in various forms for well over a decade now, usually resurging in bear markets. With a pullback from the early 2024 bull market, investor sentiment has renewed this age-old debate.

The issue seems to be nebulously defined by the following:

  1. Historically, infrastructure has generated the highest returns for crypto investors.
  2. Infrastructure is the core constraint holding applications back.
  3. Crypto has found few consumer use cases with sustained product-market fit.

So far, the vast majority of investment in the space has gone into blockchain infrastructure. Investors have poured billions of dollars into Layer 1s, Layer 2s, oracles, and other pieces of infrastructure. These investments have performed exceptionally well. For example, ETH and LINK grew by 1,634% and 806% over the past five years, compared to 87% growth in the S&P 500 over the same period.

Due to these returns, investors continue to pour capital into infrastructure through the present day, recently investing $225 million into the parallelized EVM, Monad, and $100 million into the Proof-of-Liquidity based L1 Berachain.

However, the purpose of infrastructure is to support end-user applications. Without applications, value will not ultimately accrue to the infrastructure layers. Yet, since the launch of Ethereum in July 2015, the industry has not seen the proliferation of killer use cases that more advanced infrastructure was supposed to enable. So far, crypto has shown product-market fit as a store-of-value in Bitcoin, payment rails for international transactions through stablecoins, and a venue for speculation. These use cases existed ten years ago, before the influx of venture capital into infrastructure.

Within the “infra vs app” debate, the pro-infra camp argues that insufficient infrastructure remains the core constraint of the success of crypto. Applications cannot scale without a stronger foundation to compete with existing web2 platforms.

In contrast, the pro-app camp argues that the market is over-saturated with unused infrastructure. The core constraint is developer talent being funneled into building infrastructure instead of applications. Venture Capitalists must incentivize developers to build applications rather than continue to pile money into excessive infrastructure with no demand.

These arguments, then, beg the question: What constraints actually prevent applications from being developed and brought to market?

Analyst Discussion

Dylan

To answer this question, we must first understand the potential use cases of crypto.

Two broad categories of use cases exist:

  • Decentralized versions of existing web2 use cases.
  • New use cases that were not possible before blockchain.

Consumer applications like social media, payments, and gaming are in the first category. Most of these platforms’ users value UX and performance above the benefits of decentralization. To overcome switching costs, dApps must offer UX on par with their web2 equivalent. For these sorts of use cases, infrastructure has been a core constraint. Take decentralized social media platforms. Before the development of high throughput L1s like Solana or Ethereum L2s like Base and Optimism, creating a scalable on-chain social media platform was not viable. Transactions per second (TPS) capacity was too low, and transaction fees were too high to sustain a high-interaction platform.

Since the development of infrastructure that improved these scalability problems, we’ve seen consumer applications start to gain success. For example, the DeSoc protocol, Farcaster, has demonstrated potentially one of the first social media success stories, growing from around 2,000 daily users at the beginning of January to over 70,000 daily users by July 2024. Certain applications like decentralized gaming face even more scalability constraints, needing additional infrastructure to compete with web2 games.

In the second category are highly ambitious and creative use cases, most of which have yet to be attempted. These use cases are not possible without blockchain technology. As a result, there is no precedent to build these businesses off of, making them experimental. For example, the Glow protocol is a DePIN project that incentivizes deploying solar farms to produce high additionality carbon credits. Before blockchains and DePIN, it was not possible to coordinate the decentralized deployment of solar farms in a scalable way. While such a use case has the potential to be a “killer app,” the lack of precedent for this model compared to proven infrastructure makes investors more averse to taking on additional risk.

The core constraints for applications are both infrastructure and risk aversion among investors towards untested applications.

So, what explains the lack of “killer apps?” Too much capital has gone into undifferentiated infrastructure, competing with existing infrastructure, rather than moving the needle on further scaling blockchain capabilities. Capital should be allocated to both higher-risk experimental applications and high-impact infrastructure.

However, due to the opportunity for quick profits from high FDV infra token allocations, investors need to be incentivized to change their current behavior.

Kinji

TLDR: Infrastructure development has clear demands, while application development faces higher risks due to subjective demand, resulting in applications launching at lower FDVs.

In my view, the dislocation between infrastructure and application development is partially due to the state of demand within the industry. For infrastructure developers, the current demands are fairly clear-cut: increase transaction speed, lower costs, and ensure a path to reasonable decentralization. Therefore, the path to success largely involves solving technological problems. By creating better technology, it's theoretically easy to convince existing users to migrate to your tech stack. In summary, while building infrastructure products is challenging, the path to success is straightforward: solve a technology problem, ensure it leads to meaningful improvement, and assess the market size unlocked by solving this problem.

In contrast, the demand drivers for applications are significantly murkier. In my view, crypto has only a few established use cases: decentralized store of value, digital dollars (stablecoins), and speculation. The first two are largely dominated by incumbents and offer limited opportunities for new protocols. This leaves prospective applications with two options: compete in the speculation vertical or attempt to build a new vertical with no precedent for demand. Competing in speculation has seen recent successes (e.g., Polymarket and US elections, Pendle and future yield, Ethena and funding rates). However, finding new speculative opportunities unique to crypto or better enabled onchain is increasingly difficult. Moreover, even if a new speculation opportunity is identified, success depends on others also wanting to speculate on it, introducing inherent subjectivity risk absent in infrastructure, where there's a consensus on the demand for lower fees and faster transactions.

Building an entirely new vertical faces even greater subjectivity risk. We know people are willing to speculate onchain, but what else are they willing to do? Understanding consumer demand is challenging enough without blockchain constraints, hence why most startups fail.

To summarize, applications carry more risk due to greater subjectivity in demand, which should technically offer higher upside. However, this is not reflected for private investors, as infrastructure products consistently launch at much higher fully diluted valuations (FDVs) than applications. Given the higher FDVs are we destined to see infrastructure funding outpace applications forever? I argue against this with two potential reasons:

  • Returning to the three key questions for investing in infrastructure (Can this technology problem be solved? Does solving this problem lead to meaningful improvement? How large of a market is unlocked?), I believe the answer to the second question will increasingly become "no" as winners and losers are determined from the current wave of infrastructure development. Once markets establish criteria for evaluating meaningful improvements, they can filter out copy-paste infrastructure products, leading to lower valuations for these products and more selective infrastructure funding, potentially making application investment more enticing.
  • I believe in the power of permissionless innovation and that someone, someday will discover a new source of demand at the application level. This would lower the subjectivity risk, boost valuations in liquid markets for these applications, and incentivize VCs to allocate more to new applications that service this demand.

Seth

Dylan and Kinji highlight valid points and criticisms, and I largely agree with Kinji’s line of reasoning. In previous cycles, numerous applications (that have received VC funding) have come and gone, leaving behind a trail of reasons for their failures. Beyond specific execution misses, a common point of contention between applications and infrastructure has been the lack of low-cost, high-throughput infrastructure on which to develop applications. If an investor is optimistic about the future of onchain applications, then it’s reasonable to fund infrastructure that resolves those points of contention.

Now, to Kinji’s point, consumer applications have a much more nebulous set of “demands” to work through. To get a sense of product-market fit, applications generally need to cycle through many loops of feature release, customer feedback collection and analysis, new development, and then new releases. This is challenging in an onchain setting for numerous reasons:

  • Products and features are likely deployed as smart contracts. Deploying new contracts based on consumer feedback or internal testing may require users to migrate funds, which creates poor UX. On the other hand, widely upgradeable smart contracts may artificially limit an application's user base due to the need to inherently trust the developers of the application.
  • User lock-in, or, more generally, moats from the Web2 domain, do not cleanly map onto the onchain setting.
  • Many applications only allow users to “sign in” with their wallets, leaving little room for customer outreach (i.e., no clear platform to leverage for connecting with customers).
  • Incentive campaigns can lead to inorganic or noisy product usage, making it unclear what (if any) value consumers identify in a product. For onchain applications, this is usually more of an issue relative to Web2 applications as the incentives rolled out onchain are generally much more liquid (e.g., token rewards are often easily traded for stablecoins, whereas airline miles don’t have a clear secondary market).

Stacking up the risk/rewards calculations, along with the relative time horizons on which “success” will be judged, infrastructure investments will likely make more sense. We can see this reflected in the FDV’s of the top tokens. Infrastructure has a much longer “acceptable” time horizon to actualize growth, whereas consumer onchain apps have a much more contracted timeline for success with a smaller market of users.

However, applications that can morph into platforms and functionally serve more as infrastructure is enticing from a valuation and investment perspective. Uniswap has, to date, been the best example of this path. Uniswap and its upcoming deployments will serve more as liquidity infrastructure rather than a singleton application connecting demand and supply.

Identifying these opportunities and investing in founders who can execute the vision is extremely challenging. While Uniswap is likely the best example here, it is also one of the only examples. To that end, while the application layer may see marginal funding improvements, infrastructure will likely remain dominant in absolute funding size for the foreseeable future.
