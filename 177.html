The Week In Crypto Policy: A Bitcoin Interview

George Leonardo

a day ago   10 min read

Key Insights

  • Bitcoin mining may incentivize renewable energy development by acting as a reliable base load whereby miners purchase excess green energy where there is a lack of demand, thus making green energy production profitable where it otherwise might not be.
  • Based on Bitcoin Policy Institute's meetings with policymakers and their staffs, there is still a learning curve for lawmakers to overcome when it comes to having a nuanced understanding of Bitcoin and crypto generally, but there has been considerable progress in the last few years on the education front.
  • One way to address Congress's concerns about illicit finance in crypto without hampering private, permissionless nature of blockchains would be to work with law enforcement to invest in and improve their blockchain analysis departments
  • Creating a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve in the U.S. has been picking up a lot of steam in D.C, but the details will likely be a significant source of contention politically.

Introduction

Grant McCarty is the Co-Executive Director of the Bitcoin Policy Institute, a nonpartisan, nonprofit organization researching policy and societal implications of Bitcoin and emerging monetary networks.

The questions below were aimed at better understanding Bitcoin's value proposition and related policy issues, from Proof-of-Work (PoW) mining to creating a strategic Bitcoin reserve. The interview has only been edited for formatting purposes, with certain sections bolded for emphasis.

Q & A

Question 1: What is the central mission of the Bitcoin Policy Institute and what are some key initiatives you are currently working on?

A: Bitcoin Policy Institutes central mission is to explore the intersection of digital assets and existing U.S. public policy interests. We work with dozens of experts across a number of different disciplines and across the partisan spectrum who each conduct original research and policy analysis that looks at Bitcoin in relation to national security, environmental impact, human rights, and more. To put it as simply as possible, we want to figure out how the U.S. can maximize the benefits of Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies while also minimizing the risks.

Some projects worth mentioning:

  • We recently published a report on Bitcoin mining, using real data from miners in the U.S. to compare their carbon emissions in relation to other kinds of data centers.
  • Were producing a policy paper that digs into the concept of the U.S. adopting a strategic bitcoin reserve, based on the recent proposals from various Presidential candidates and members of Congress. That should be completed in the next few weeks.
  • Each year in April, we host the Bitcoin Policy Summit in Washington, D.C., so were already planning that for 2025.
  • Were continuing to do day-to-day education on the Hill! The interest in crypto is at an all-time high in D.C., and this is true across the aisle, so were taking meetings every single day trying to help people wrap their heads around anything in this space that they want to learn more about.

Question 2: What do you think is Bitcoins fundamental value proposition? In other words, why should individuals and policymakers care about Bitcoin?

A: The fascinating thing about Bitcoin, from my perspective, is that it isnt really any one thing. For example, it can be a savings vehicle, a store of value analogous to digital gold. Id say thats the strongest, most proven use case for Bitcoin currently.

But it can also be a medium of exchange, effectively functioning as money for people around the world whose currencies arent ideal to transact with. Furthermore, there is the concept of Bitcoin as a protocol; essentially, the Bitcoin network is governed by a set of rules that anyone can use to build on top of (like the TCP/IP protocol we know of as the internet), which opens the door for things like decentralized identity, stablecoins, etc. all being built on the Bitcoin network. Its possible that Bitcoins best use case hasnt even been invented yet.

At the end of the day though, the best argument I can make for Bitcoin is this: its the most secure computer network in the entire world, it doesnt break, and its accessible to anyone around the world with an internet connection or access to a cell phone. It does a few things better than any other technology in the world, and it provides value to millions of people around the world every single day. If you are one of those people, then great! If you arent, then no harm, no foul.

Question 3: We hear a lot about Bitcoin as a hedge against fiat currency depreciation over the long-term, but what are some other potential benefits or use cases for Bitcoin that you would like more policymakers and individuals to be aware of?

A: One underrated feature of Bitcoin is its proof of work consensus mechanism. Mining bitcoin (using the proof-of-work process to secure the Bitcoin network) requires a lot of energy, and many people often view that energy usage as a waste. But an alternative perspective has recently been taking hold in the academic community, one that views bitcoin mining through the lens of monetization of excess energy.

The argument is essentially that in order to meet our energy security and energy sustainability goals in the U.S., we need to generate a significantly higher amount of renewable energy in the coming years. But currently, building out additional renewable energy infrastructure is unprofitable in many areas due to lack of demand. Bitcoin mining offers a potential solution to this lack of demand, acting as a reliable base load for renewable energy projects and making it profitable to increase green energy production in the US.

Since theres no other buyer of energy with the same properties as Bitcoin, many environmental advocates are beginning to see it as a potential game changer in the fight for a more sustainable future.

Question 4: From an education standpoint, do you find that Members of Congress, regulators, and their staff understand Bitcoin and why constituents care about it? Or do you find there is still a learning curve to overcome?

A: Theres certainly a learning curve with Bitcoin, just as there is with any new technology. One of the key challenges is just how broad and varied the entire crypto ecosystem really is. There are literally tens of thousands of different crypto projects that have ever been created, and they each have different properties that warrant unique consideration.

Bitcoin is different from stablecoins, which are different from DeFi, which is different from things like Shiba Inu Coin or Garfield Token. A lot of crypto projects actually are scams, or unregistered securities / investment contracts, or well-intentioned projects that no one uses anymore. But there are some diamonds in the rough, and policymakers are slowly coming around to that.

Just looking at where were at with lawmakers understanding of this technology today, even compared to only a few years ago, I am blown away by how far weve come. Theres still so much work to do! But were closer to being in a good place than most people might initially believe.

Question 5: One of the key concerns policymakers have raised against Bitcoin is the amount of electricity that is consumed through the networks proof-of-work consensus mechanism. For example, weve seen President Biden propose a digital asset mining tax as part of his budget a few years in a row now, and the Energy Information Agency wants to collect information from miners about energy usage.

How would you respond to those who are concerned about the energy usage behind proof-of-work mining?

A: I think my answer to Question 3 is probably the best response to this concern. But the other thing Ill mention is that if you are concerned about energy usage and the environmental impact of energy consumption in the U.S., then I encourage you to go after the root of the problem.

If a bitcoin mining facility is connected to an energy grid in Iowa that uses 75% coal and 25% wind energy, then every consumer of energy on that grid is using on average 75% coal and 25% wind. Bitcoin mining isnt really unique in that regard; its using the same energy stack as the home five miles away thats running the dishwasher and powering a Playstation 5.

If you want the energy grid to use more wind energy and less coal, then the answer isnt to go after bitcoin mining facilities. That isnt going to fix the problem youre trying to solve. The answer is to somehow incentivize the energy providers to change the type of energy being consumed, and that requires an entirely different solution than simply telling bitcoin miners to go away.

Question 6: Another key concern Members of Congress have raised regarding Bitcoin (and crypto more broadly) is that bitcoin can be used to facilitate illicit financial transactions. How do you think policymakers should address the illicit finance concerns around Bitcoin without hampering the permissionless nature of the network?

A: Unfortunately, my answer here probably wont be able to go into enough detail to really be useful, and I dont want to resort to platitudes, but rest assured that this is one of the key topics of conversation that comes up when were engaging with members of Congress, law enforcement, and the intelligence community.

One key principle we advocate for is to go after bad actors, but dont limit the use of free and open-source software. If code is speech, then that speech should be Constitutionally protected. The use of that speech for illicit purposes is not protected; but its important that we understand these are still two separate things. In practical terms, as an example, this means that we shouldnt be punishing creators of open-source non-custodial bitcoin wallets simply for creating this software, even if someone chooses to use that software for an illicit activity.

If people actually want to stop criminals from using Bitcoin, one of the best things they could do is work with law enforcement to invest in and improve their blockchain analysis departments. Bitcoin transactions exist on a public ledger; the information is there to catch the bad guys if you look hard enough and have the right tools.

Question 7: What are some key Bitcoin-specific policy items you and BPI are focused on this year and over the next Congress? Are there any specific bills you would like to see passed this year?

A: The idea of creating a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve in the U.S. has been picking up a lot of steam in D.C. lately, from both Democrats and Republicans. As I mentioned earlier, BPI will be putting together a full policy report on this topic.

In general, we think this would be a prudent strategy for the US to pursue. The US already holds various strategic reserves, including gold. If you view bitcoin as digital gold, then perhaps it follows that the U.S. should also hold a bit of bitcoin to help reinforce American monetary dominance, counter our geopolitical adversaries, and build long-term financial resilience in case of certain tail-risk scenarios. You actually dont really even have to like or believe in Bitcoin for this to make sense from an insurance/risk perspective.

That said, I imagine the details will be a significant source of contention politically. How much bitcoin do we buy? Why only Bitcoin, and not something like Ethereum too? Should we sell gold to be able to pay for the bitcoin? Those debates should be fun.

Question 8: With the election just a few months away, what advice would you give readers who would like to see more pro-Bitcoin Members of Congress get elected in November? Relatedly, how can they be most effective in moving the needle with policymakers in D.C.?

A: Be loud. Show politicians that you are a real person who really cares. Be careful not to confirm peoples worst assumptions about Bitcoin and crypto; surprise them if you can.

Are you a progressive voter who owns some bitcoin? Take five minutes to email your Senator to let them know! Are you a Republican who cares about transitioning to more renewable energy, and you think bitcoin mining has a role to play in that transition? Call your Representatives D.C. office to tell them that! Are you an independent voter who views crypto policy as a deciding factor in your Presidential decision this year? Email the Harris campaign, the Trump campaign, the DNC, and the RNC to tell them that!

At a certain point, the loudest people are the ones who politicians respond to. Be respectful and persistent, and you will be heard.
