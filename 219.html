The Week in Crypto Policy: Tigran Release Resolution Advances, OCP 2.0, SEC Oversight Hearing

George Leonardo

Sep 27, 2024   10 min read

Key Insights

  • The House Foreign Affairs Committee advanced a resolution urging the Nigerian government to release Tigran Gambaryan, the U.S. citizen, Binance executive, and former IRS employee, who lawmakers say is being wrongfully detained.
  • The House Financial Services Committee held an SEC oversight hearing, covering regulatory clarity, stablecoins, NFTs, special purpose broker dealers, and more.
  • Filings in the Silvergate bankruptcy proceedings suggest increased supervisory pressure from federal banking regulators forced the bank to liquidate.
  • VP Harris's new economic platform says she will encourage innovative technologies like AI and digital assets while protecting our consumers and investors.
  • A federal judge denied a Tornado Cash developer's motion to dismiss criminal charges, ruling that control is not a necessary component of an illegal money transmitting offense, allowing the case to proceed to trial in December.

HFAC Passes Tigran Resolution

On Tuesday, the House Foreign Affairs Committee advanced a resolution urging the Nigerian government to release Tigran Gambaryan in a bipartisan voice vote.

The resolution:

  • Urges the Nigerian government to immediately release Tigran and ensure proper access to medical care, consular services, and counsel.
  • Urges the United States government to use all available resources to secure his release and declare him wrongfully detained.

Rep. Richard McCormick (R-GA) & Rep French Hill (R-AR) are the lead sponsors. There are 41 additional sponsors.

  • Text: H. Res. 1348.
  • Watch Markup (discussion/vote starts around 3:18:22).
  • More on Tigran's case by Gary Weinstein in Forbes.

House SEC Hearing

On Tuesday, all five SEC commissioners testified before the House Financial Services Committee (HFSC).

TLDW

Like previous hearings, pro-crypto members criticized Chair Gensler for prioritizing enforcement over transparent rulemaking and placing politics above the SECs statutory mission. Unlike previous hearings, this time, Republican Commissioners Hester Peirce and Mark Uyeda were present to join these members in criticizing the Commission's track record on digital assets. Ranking Member Maxine Waters (D-NC) talked stablecoins, Rep. Stephen Lynch (D-MA) defended Chair Gensler's enforcement record, and Rep. Bill Foster (D-IL) briefly raised concerns about anonymous self-hosted wallets. But for the most part, Democrats focused questions on non-crypto related issues.

Crypto Highlights

Stablecoins

Ranking Member Maxine Waters said shes committed to getting bipartisan stablecoin legislation done this year. But, she also emphasized that the Federal Reserve should play a leading role overseeing issuers - the key point of contention with Republicans - so it's unclear to what extent this signals movement.

SAB 121

Members are increasingly focusing SAB 121 attacks on the SEC's process of granting one-off relief via consultations with the SEC's Chief Accounting Officer (CAO), rather than repealing the rule outright.

  • Chair Gensler defended SAB 121 as good accounting that protects customers, particularly in bankruptcy.
  • According to his testimony, if someone shows (1) customer funds are held in a bankruptcy remote entity and (2) the digital assets are truly the customers, then its not a liability.
  • He also rejected assertions that the CAO consultation process is new or unique to SAB 121.

NFTs

Members criticized SEC Commissioners for bringing enforcement actions against NFT creators.

For example, Rep. Ritchie Torres (D-NY) asked Chair Gensler to explain why buying a Yankees ticket is not a security transaction, but buying an NFT that offers you access to an animated web series is.

  • When Gensler evaded the question, Torres criticized the Chair's theory of what constitutes an investment contract as lacking anything resembling a limiting principle.

Rep. Torres also questioned Commissioner Crenshaw on whether requiring the registration of artwork, or requiring creators to destroy art (as required in the Stoner Cats case), raised First Amendment concerns.

  • Crenshaw said she's not a First Amendment expert.

Rep. William Timmons (R-SC) highlighted his draft bill to clarify that certain NFTs and transactions in them are not subject to federal securities laws.

  • Text: New Frontiers in Technology Act.

SPBD

The SEC's registration process for digital asset special purpose broker dealers (SPBDs) is gaining more attention, with Reps. French Hill (R-AR), Warren Davidson (R-OH), John Rose (R-TN), and Erin Houchin (R-ID) asking questions on the topic, like:

  • (1) why have so few SPBDs been approved and/or
  • (2) what is the significance of approval, given that SPBDs are unable to custody non-security digital assets and the lack of clarity around when digital assets are offered or sold as securities.

Legislation to codify criteria for brokers and dealers to register as SPBDs was noticed for the hearing (and last week's on DeFi).

  • Text: SPBD Bill.

VP Harris's Digital Asset Comments

VP Harris recently told a group of donors on Wall Street that she will encourage innovative technologies like AI and digital assets while protecting our consumers and investors.

Reps. Tom Emmer (R-MN) and Wiley Nickel (D-NC) pressed Gensler to opine on these comments, but Chair Gensler rejected any inconsistency between VP Harriss statements and his approach, while emphasizing consumer protections.

For example, Rep. Nickel asked every Commissioner if they agreed with VP Harriss statement. Their responses:

  • Uyeda: Yes.
  • Peirce: Yes.
  • Lizrraga: Yes.
  • Crenshaw: Yes.
  • Gensler: Protecting the investor. I agree with that for sure. Promoting innovation, yes, but protecting investors.
    • Rep. Nickel (following up): Yes or no?
    • Gensler: Yes, protect the investors, thats what we are doing.

Silvergate Bankruptcy Filing

Late last week, Nic Carter unveiled the latest evidence that federal banking regulators have pressured banks to limit their crypto clients - and possibly forced one to liquidate.

Background

Silvergate Bank was one of several banks forced to wind down operations during last year's banking crisis - opting for voluntary liquidation in March 2023.

Silvergate focused its business on crypto clients, with crypto client deposits representing 90% of total customer deposits in late 2022. (See CRS Report).

The extent to which interest rate risk, crypto customer deposit withdrawals, connections to FTX, inadequate AML controls, or regulatory pressure was the primary cause that spurred the bank's liquidation remains a topic of contentious debate.

New Bankruptcy Filing

Last Wednesday, Silvergate Bank's parent company, Silvergate Capital Corp. (SCC) filed first day motions in its Chapter 11 bankruptcy proceedings.

Most notably, Elaine Hetrick, Chief Administrative Officer of SCC, filed a declaration laying out her view of events leading to Silvergate's decision to liquidate.

  • Hetrick Declaration (Decl.).

According to Hetrick, Silvergate faced headwinds from a rapid rise in interest rates and significant customer withdrawals following FTX's collapse, but the bank successfully navigated these risks.

  • See, e.g., Decl. at 5-6:
  • The crisis of confidence across the digital asset industry, along with similar problems faced by banks across the country, caused a 'run on the bank.' Silvergate Bank was able to manage this bank run and pay all its depositors as they withdrew funds as a result of its liquidity risk management and planning.

Hetrick points to increased supervisory pressure as the ultimate catalyst for why the bank's business model was no longer viable:

It's unclear what increased supervisory pressure fully entails, as Henrick says she's prohibited by law from explaining.

  • See Decl. FN 10, citing FN 5, stating:
  • Silvergate is prohibited by law from disclosing confidential supervisory information, which broadly includes correspondence and communications with the Federal Banking Regulatory Agencies as well as reports of supervisory examination.

However, Hetrick does cite public actions taken by federal banking regulators, including:

  • The First Joint Statement by federal banking regulators publicly advising of significant safety and soundness concerns with business models that are concentrated in crypto-asset-related activities or have concentrated exposure to the crypto-asset sector on Jan. 3, 2023. Id. at 8.
  • The Second Joint Statement by federal banking regulators highlighting that banking organizations may face heightened liquidity risk from having concentration in crypto-asset-related entities as depository customers on Feb. 23, 2023. Id. at 9.
  • The FDIC's decision to exclude Signature Bank's digital asset customers' deposits (totaling $4 billion) in its sale to Flagstar Bank. Id. at 10.

Hetrick sums up these actions as follows:

  • This public signaling and sudden regulatory shift made clear that, at least as of the first quarter of 2023, the Federal Bank Regulatory Agencies would not tolerate banks with significant concentrations of digital asset customers, ultimately preventing Silvergate Bank from continuing its digital asset focused business model. Id. at 10-11 (emphasis added).

What's Next?

Will these revelations spur Congress to investigative what increased supervisory pressures were applied to Silvergate or are being applied to other banks? Or pass legislation to tighten up federal banking regulators' discretion to pressure banks to avoid politically disfavored businesses?

Quick Hits

Congress

Legislation

  • Rep. Warren Davidson (R-OH) intro'd a bill to direct the CFTC and SEC to study potential benefits and risks related to DeFi.
    • Text: Evaluating DeFi Opportunities Act.

Letters

  • Rescind SAB 121: Senator Cynthia Lummis (R-WY) and HFSC Chair Patrick McHenry (R-NC) lead another letter urging Chair Gensler to rescind SAB 121.
    • Letter.
  • SAB 121 Doc Requests: HFSC Chair McHenry and subcomm chairs Reps. Bill Huizenga (R-MI), French Hill (R-AR), and Andy Barr (R-KY) are requesting interagency communications between the Fed, FDIC, OCC, and SEC regarding SAB 121.
    • Letter.

Courts

  • U.S. v. Storm: The DOJ's case against Roman Storm, a co-founder of Tornado Cash, will proceed to trial after Judge Failla denied his motion to dismiss. Trial is scheduled for December.
    • Charges include conspiracies to operate an unlicensed money transmitting business, commit money laundering, and violate U.S. sanctions law (IEEPA).
      • DeFi Education Fund's Chief Counsel, Amanda Tuminelli, breaks down the ruling from the bench.
      • Docket.
    • Judge Failla: The court agrees with the government that control is not a necessary component of a 1960 offense. A control requirement is not in the statute and this court is not going to read it in.
    • Tie to Congress: In May, Senators Cynthia Lummis (R-WY) & Ron Wyden (D-OR) wrote Attorney General Garland on this very issue, urging the DOJ to discard this flawed interpretation of Section 1960, citing the clear intent of Congress and guidance from FinCEN indicating that taking control of users' assets is a key element of 1960.
  • SEC v. TrueCoin LLC: The SEC charged two entities with defrauding investors regarding a stablecoin investment program and offering unregistered securities.
    • Press Release & Complaint.
  • Coinbase v. SEC: The Third Circuit held oral argument's in Coinbase's case seeking to compel the SEC to issue workable rules for digital assets.
    • Summary: Court questions SEC's 'vacuous' denial of Coinbase rulemaking petition (Martin Young; Cointelegraph).
    • Listen: here.

Admin

  • Treasury said it's planning to finalize rules for non-custodial digital asset brokers this year.
    • In July, Treasury published final rules applicable to custodial digital asset brokers (e.g., exchanges, hosted wallets, kiosks), while noting it intended to expeditiously issue separate final regulations describing information reporting rules for non-custodial industry participants.
    • TBD to what extent the final non-custodial rules will mirror those in the proposed rule.
    • Will this motivate Congress to move on the bipartisan, bicameral Keep Innovation in America Act?
  • BNY Approved by SEC for Crypto Custody Beyond ETFs, Gensler Says (Bloomberg)

2024 Election

Presidential

  • VP Harris unveiled her New Way Forward economic platform, stating she will encourage innovative technologies like AI and digital assets while protecting our consumers and investors.
  • In a speech previewing the platform, she said we will remain dominant in AI and quantum computing, blockchain and other emerging technologies.
    • As the Biden-Harris Admin has used similar language re promoting innovation, protecting consumers, and reinforcing American leadership (see, e.g., Executive Order on Ensuring Responsible Development of Digital Assets (March 2022) and the Statement of Administration Policy re FIT 21 (May 2024)), it's unclear to what extent this signals a meaningful shift from the current Administration's stance.
    • Keeping eyes out for comments on specific polices of her Admin like SEC's SAB 121, DOJ arguing money transmitter laws apply to non-custodial crypto service providers, OCP 2.0, etc.

Congress

  • If Democrats win the House (63% chance per Polymarket), Rep. Maxine Waters (D-CA) would most likely become House Financial Services Chair. She previews her priorities and thoughts on crypto, telling Punchbowl crypto is inevitable and Politico crypto is the future.
