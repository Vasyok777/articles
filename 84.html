The Road Ahead for Rollups and EIP4844
Seth Bloomberg

Jan 19, 2024 ⋅  10 min read

Key Insights

  • The rollout of EIP4844, likely in H1 2024, will provide rollups with potentially 10x cheaper cost to use Ethereum as a Data Availability (DA) solution.
  • The operating cost reduction rollups gain will likely create significant decreases in top-line revenue, as well. Rollups generate revenues from transaction fees, and competitive forces to drive fees as low as possible will be primarily responsible for these revenue reductions.
  • Rollups can leverage a lower operating cost landscape to attract new users or new rollups to launch with their tech stacks, offsetting the loss of current revenue levels with net new user activity.
  • Even with the prospects of high growth potential, rollup native tokens will need to generate creative solutions to attain a monetary premium that other smart contract platform tokens currently hold.

Rollups have long been touted as one of the primary methods of scaling the Ethereum network. Now, with numerous rollups live and in production, users are able to take advantage of the lower transaction costs these platforms offer. Rollups routinely offer 10x cheaper fees than Ethereum mainnet.

The lower transaction fees offered by rollups are due to the fact that they perform the computation and transaction execution that the Ethereum mainnet typically handles. Once transactions are processed on rollups, they must post the associated transaction data to Ethereum, which functions as the DA layer. Storing data on Ethereum is expensive, much to the chagrin of rollups. Over the course of 2023, Arbitrum alone spent over $46 million in data publishing costs to Ethereum.

Addressing this large and growing cost for rollups is EIP4844, or proto-danksharding. EIP4844 is a first step towards reducing Ethereum DA costs, and is likely to land on Ethereum mainnet in H1 2024.

The immediate impact of proto-danksharding will be lower operating costs for rollups that opt into using it. The second-order effects will likely be felt by users in the form of even lower transaction fees and also by rollups themselves as their overall business strategies and models adjust to this new DA regime.

Proto-Danksharding: EIP4844

Ethereum offers a single, general solution and fee market for data storage, which all entities (e.g., rollups, applications, end-users, etc.) on Ethereum share. However, rollups and their growing demand for data storage express the need for a less homogenous data storage marketplace.

In December, rollups published nearly 22,000 MB of data down to Ethereum and paid over $32 million in storage costs, a figure expected to grow as more rollups come to production.

Blob Data Storage & Independent Fee Markets

Proto-danksharding introduces a separate, independent data storage solution (i.e., data blobs) and fee market for rollups to leverage that is separate from the execution layer where normal transactions are processed. The cost of accessing data blobs is designed to be significantly cheaper than the current one-size-fits-all solution, with some estimates citing a 10x decrease fee reduction for rollups. Data blobs use the pricing mechanism introduced by EIP1559 so that the price per data blob increases as demand exceeds the target utilization.

The implementation of proto-danksharding serves as both an immediate solution to cost challenges faced by rollups as well as paves the path for further scaling upgrades on the horizon. Once deployed, rollups will have access to a target value of 3 data blobs, or ~0.375 MB per block. For reference, Arbitrum and Optimism, in aggregate, contribute ~0.0225 MB per block under the current design.

Down the road, the goal is to introduce full danksharding, which, among many other advancements, will add ~16 MB of data per block for rollups to use.

Rollup Economic Outlook Post EIP4844

At a basic level, rollups generate revenue by charging transaction fees and incur data publishing costs. The difference between the collected transaction fees and data publishing costs can be thought of as the net onchain profit of a rollup. ZK rollups must also publish ZK proofs to a settlement layer, which is an additional, recurring cost in addition to the offchain proof generation cost. To date, rollups have been money printers.

The top rollups routinely rake in millions of dollars monthly from transaction fees and typically have onchain profits ranging between $500,000 - $2 million.

First-order Effects: Structural Shift in Rollup Costs

The introduction of proto-danksharding will unquestionably reduce the average costs rollups incur for data publishing. In December, all rollups combined wrote down ~22,000 MB of data to Ethereum and paid ~$34 million, reducing to ~$1500 per MB.

Demand to access data blobs will naturally increase as more rollups or other interested entities (e.g., inscription creators) deploy. Since data blobs use an EIP1559 pricing model, the real costs for rollups will vary with demand spikes, but until the target values are exceeded, blob pricing will stay at its minimum possible value (in ETH terms). Again, using the December rollup data publishing figures along with the target blob utilization, current rollups will account for ~27% of the 3 data blob per block target.

From the top-line perspective, a significant, structural reduction in rollup costs represents a large tailwind for rollups across the board. However, the data publishing costs of rollups are intertwined with the revenues they generate.

Second-order Effects: Structural Shift in Rollup Revenues

Rollups take into account the current costs of publishing data on Ethereum when updating their transaction fees. Intuitively, then, as the costs of publishing data on Ethereum are vastly reduced by EIP4844, this should proportionally decrease the transaction fee charged to rollup users. Since rollup transaction fees equate to rollup revenues, top-line revenues should take a significant hit.

The underlying assumption in this line of reasoning is that rollups will pass through all of their cost savings onto their end-users. Why does this assumption hold weight? Rollups are competing for users; all else equal, users should gravitate towards the cheapest rollup. Albeit there is more nuance to unpack, the Base team, for instance, has indicated it aims for sub-penny transaction fees, something that, in the short term, is only viable by passing on cost savings to users.

Stimulating the Rollup Economy

With cost and, likely, revenue reductions stemming from EIP4844, rollups will need to drive increased activity within their networks to maintain current profit levels. This may come in the form of more rollup deployments or from increasing the user base on the current main rollup, such as zkSync Era or Optimism Mainnet.

More Rollups or More Users: Why not both?

Most rollups have tech stacks that can be leveraged by other teams looking to build their own rollups – Optimism’s OP Stack, Arbitrum’s Orbit solution, and zkSync’s ZK Stack.

For each of these tech stacks, the underlying general strategy is to enable other rollups to easily deploy and opt into leveraging shared infrastructure (e.g., a bridge, a sequencer or a set of sequencers). Optimism’s “Law of Chains” clearly illustrates this strategy.

Now, with EIP4844 on the near horizon, deploying a rollup using one of these tech stacks and relying on Ethereum as a DA layer will be much more cost-effective than the current status quo.

However, in a practical setting, EIP4844 is not a silver bullet. While it does create cheaper DA and higher throughput than current Ethereum levels, alternative DA solutions also offer appealing solutions. Celestia, for instance, offers nearly a ~200x improvement in DA throughput (measured in MB/second) relative to the initial EIP4844 configuration.

Even within each rollup tech stack, one can find alternative DA solutions; Arbitrum has developed its AnyTrust Data Availability Committee (DAC) and zkSync’s zkPorter DAC.

The Arbitrum Orbit chain, Xai, is one of the early adopters of Arbitrum’s AnyTrust DAC. The Xai networks’ XAI token is used as a gas token, among other things. Other chains that accept the tradeoff between higher trust assumptions for DA and lower DA costs will likely follow a similar path, siphoning away value from the underlying tokens such as ETH or TIA in Celestia’s case.

For the current set of dominant rollups, the overarching theme here is twofold:

  • Large, existing rollups (e.g., Optimism, Arbitrum, Base, etc) can take advantage of EIP4844 by offering lower-cost transactions on their chains. Lower costs can also enable new applications to launch and thereby attract new users.
  • With cheaper DA solutions (either from EIP4844 or alternative DA options), new rollups can now more cost-effectively deploy chains using rollup tech stacks and opt into using shared infrastructure.

Both of these approaches serve the goal of adding new users, more activity, and liquidity to existing rollup networks.

Reassessing Rollup Business Models

Fundamentally, rollups have functioned as smart contract platforms, similar to Ethereum or Solana. Examining the fees generated from rollups, then, gives insight into the relative demand they’re attracting. In the short-to-medium term, whether from EIP4844 or other DA solutions, revenues will likely decline in tandem with operating costs for rollups.

Cost Savings as a Growth Catalyst

On the surface, less revenue from lower transaction fees appears bearish for rollups. However, the upside in the cost savings for rollups cannot be ignored.

More generally, whenever any business receives a structural change in its operating costs, this tends to open up doors for growth opportunities. For rollups, these opportunities should manifest themselves via new applications (e.g., onchain games), new users (e.g., users that want access to these new apps), and net new transactions (e.g., arbitrage transactions that were previously not economically viable).

From this perspective, assessing rollups primarily from a fee-generation lens is short-sighted. Many rollups, such as the ZK variety, just launched in 2023 and, as is the case with the majority of crypto, are pre product-market fit — it’s still in the early innings for rollups.

Lack of Monetary Premium for Rollup Tokens

Many native tokens of smart contract platforms have arguably achieved a “money” status. This,  in turn, earns them a monetary premium in valuation exercises.

Applying a firm definition of money in this context is a nebulous task, but some common heuristics can be applied:

  • Gas Token – Does the native token function as the gas token for a network?
  • Prime Collateral – Does the native token act as a prime source of collateral for the network?
  • Asset Pricing (NFTs) – Are assets, such as NFTs, priced in terms of the native token?

Rollup tokens, such as ARB and OP, generally fail each heuristic check noted above. Neither ARB nor OP serves as the gas token on their networks. On Aave V3, the largest debt protocol on both Optimism and Arbitrum, ARB and OP both lag behind wstETH, ETH, and wBTC as collateral by a wide margin. NFTs are generally still priced in ETH as well.

The “money-ness” of an asset is not necessarily static; it can change over time. But, for instance, as long as rollups like Arbitrum and Optimism leverage Ethereum as the DA layer, they will use ETH over their native assets as the gas token. From the valuation perspective, rollup native tokens will likely face a continual challenge in attaining a monetary premium.

Final Thoughts

The arrival of EIP4844 is the first step within the Ethereum roadmap to bring operating cost relief to rollups. For rollups that opt into leveraging proto-danksharding, they can expect potentially 10x in cost reductions. In the immediate aftermath of EIP4844, rollups will need to fine-tune their pricing mechanisms to account for this new data fee market properly. In the longer term, competitive forces should naturally push rollups to pass through effectively all of their cost savings onto users, thereby creating a significant drop in top-line revenues.

Emerging alternative DA solutions offer orders of magnitude higher throughput and cheaper costs than the initial specifications of EIP4844. Rollups that can accept the tradeoff of lower security for lower operating costs will likely choose to leverage these alternative DA solutions, especially as they become widely integrated into Rollup-as-a-Service providers and other rollup tech stacks.

Together, the introduction of EIP4844, along with alternative DA solutions, put rollup growth strategies front and center. Rollups will need to take advantage of lower operating costs by attracting new developers to build on their current platform or to spin up a rollup using the rollups tech stack and opting into leveraging shared infrastructure.
